# Foreword, 2019

![Free Cultural Works](assets/Definition_of_Free_Cultural_Works_logo_notext.svg)

When the Creative Commons licenses were first created, especially in the wake of successive copyright law expansions, I felt excited about the flood of Free Culture franchises I anticipated.  I still am, but they haven't *quite* manifested.

To be fair, we do have a fair amount of content available under a Free Culture license.  The Blender Foundation's Open Projects are packed with a variety of interesting ideas and characters.  David Revoy's **Pepper & Carrot** has an interesting high-fantasy world and there are other, less known, web comics such as **Homem-Grilo** by Cadu Simões.  **Where Are the Joneses?** was a fully realized sitcom, despite the short episode lengths.  You can find novels and short stories of varying levels of quality and completeness.  And, of course, there is always more, though it's often difficult to discover.

If you're willing to expand into the non-free licenses, the list expands significantly, but I'm not interested in that world.

Regardless of the scope, though, very few of the properties feel like they could sustain an open-ended series in another medium, even with money and talent poured in arbitrarily.  Those that do feel like they can survive tend to build themselves around the voice and aesthetic of a single creator.  That's not to say that a Hollywood-style movie franchise should be some sort of ultimate goal for anybody's work, mind you.  But, to the extent it feels like a work would be hard to adapt and extend, the work seems like it's cutting against the grain of the modern entertainment industry.

Therefore, in true *become the crackpot you want to see in the world* spirit, the Silver Bat is an attempt to lay the foundations of a hypothetical superhero universe.  It resembles, but definitely is not, our world.  The organization has a hundred-year history, with many main and side characters at any given time.  I can't vouch for the quality and realize that the story won't work for every reader, but I think this is the step forward on building a Free Culture universe that feels like the sort of place that big-name comic books or movies might use as a setting, without being the sort of thing that a large company would rush to publish.

And there's more to this world to come.  The Luminary and his associated heroes are still out there, and I have a fairly good idea of who they are, only waiting on a story.

Whether this series continues or expands, though, it is now available to the world, under a license that allows *you* to take it in your own direction.  I will probably protect the trademark of the Silver Bat name, but the world and characters are now as much yours as mine, as long as you comply with the terms of the license.

Note that, for convenience and verisimilitude, just as I license this novel for use by others, the novel makes use of works under compatible free licenses.  Please review the **Credits** section for details.

Oh, and as a side benefit to the open license, I am presenting this as open source, with the files used to generate the book posted at <https://github.com/jcolag/SilverBat-01>.  If you see any errors, feel free to sign in and create a *pull request* for me to review.  Future versions of that **Credits** section will include people who helped with this.

# Foreword to the Second Edition

Nearly six years ago, as I write this, a novel almost accidentally fell into place.  As you might have noted in the original foreword, I started with the intent of creating a "media property" that other people could continue without it feeling strange, in contrast to the Free Culture projects that so often feel intensely personal or tied to the creator.  For example, while he has plenty of help and I have seen a ton of fan art set in the universe with the same characters, I still can't imagine the aforementioned **Pepper & Carrot** continuing in any meaningful capacity when (if?) David Revoy puts it aside, because so much of its fun derives from his interests and personality.  Likewise, I can't imagine the *Green Comet* story branching out without Jim Bowering at the heart of the project.  I don't object to those projects, and even love many of them, but they also feel like they undercut the idea of Free Culture as a quasi-collaborative effort.

To build something that wouldn't need me, I chose the most mainstream inspiration that I could find at the time - Batman, who the comics and studio constantly reinvent based on new visions - and proceeded to create a modern version of the archetype with a Free Culture aesthetic.  Here, the Silver Bat becomes a natural legacy character, the title passed down as each individual steps aside.  And it becomes less a single character than a vast underground network, where they share resources and ideas, but each agent puts their own spin on the idea, based on the needs of their community, echoing the Free Culture movement without moralizing about it; the legacy aspect echoes the adaptation involved in creation, and the sharing seems self-explanatory.  And most important for me, rather than a Zorro-style swashbuckler out for revenge, the Silver Bat's collective takes its inspiration from *The Scarlet Pimpernel* books, a "league" more interested in rescuing mass political prisoners and the oppressed.

I even spent some time thinking through the "costume," something that might look striking when necessary, especially if somebody decided to adapt the idea to another medium, but also centered on "off the rack" elements, so that nobody needed to worry where they stored their costumes.  And I also wanted something that, if this did somehow become popular, fans would recognize somebody dressed up as an agent of the League, but doing so wouldn't require purchasing much out of the ordinary, and a person could customize the idea.

At roughtly the time that I kicked around this idea, the (first) Trump administration provided me with a mass of political prisoners in the mainstream news:  Undocumented immigrants, who the administration began arresting and housing in - their word, not mine, as I recall, and tried to suggest that it didn't need to mean anything *bad* - concentration camps, not to mention the broader [Muslim bans](https://jurnal.ugm.ac.id/jsp/article/view/82148) and [family separation policies](https://otherwords.org/being-separated-from-my-child-nearly-destroyed-me/).  This dovetailed nicely with the actual best piece of *The Scarlet Pimpernel* media, **Pimpernel Smith**/**Mister V**, Leslie Howard's World War II sequel to his direct adaptation of the first book, where archeologist Horatio Smith brings a team of students to a German dig, as a pretext to raid concentration camps, which then inspired real-world imitator Raoul Wallenberg to save the lives of hundreds.

While the big picture of "kids in cages" gave this world a reason to exist, Ivanka Trump's fashion line launch, reportedly made in sweatshops, provided a more mundane reason for the organization to exist.  A gritty day-to-day story could involve working the streets (anywhere in the world) to find and free a single indentured servant.  For a more strategic story, they could work to force an employer to pay stolen wages.  And the idea could climb all the way up to sweeping stories about rescuing people in the paths of ethnic cleansing campaigns.

From there, I sketched out a loose adaptation of "the Hero's Journey" - problematic as I might find the structure, the broader structure works for an origin story - to make sure that I kept the story pointing in the right direction.  I picked out a few ideas from Free Culture and public domain works that I wanted to play with in this context, and the story poured out.  I didn't keep records other than the git repository, and after six years I don't remember many details, but I started with the intent of a NaNoWriMo-inspired 1,667 words per day (fifty thousand words for the month), and quickly found myself counting "days off" that I could take at the end as I flew past that rate.  Some combination of the protagonist, the politics, and the structure caused this to fly by in a blur.

After packaging up my prose using a fairly awkward scheme that relied on too many manual steps for my taste, I dropped the first edition on the Internet Archive, and...mostly forgot about it as I started writing my blog.  I spared one of my earliest posts to talk about and link to the book, and haven't touched it since, other than to occasionally link back to that post to remind people (including myself) that I once wrote a novel.

In retrospect, a lot of the book seems over-wrought.  I still love the idea, and can't not like the main plot of taking a team into definitely-not-Texas to liberate a secret labor camp from weirdos who love Germanic languages.  But the quotes, the poems, and so much more feel outright pretentious at best, and the mark of a raving conspiracy theorist at their worst.  Really, *dare to know*, in Latin, no less?  Did "trust no-one" feel like too much of a trademark...?

Yet somehow, six years later, we have concentration camps again.  We again have people taken off the streets and vanished to labor camps, both domestically and abroad, this time with more aggression.  We have actual genocides taking place with television cameras patiently watching.  Wage theft even seems to have started rising again, after the Biden administration started taking employers to court, and the incoming Trump administration signaled that the agencies will reserve prosecution for his perceived enemies, not to protect the people.  And so it seemed like time to maybe dust off the vigilantes.

And speaking of wide-eyed conspiracy theories, the story also has its painfully naïve moments.  In my drive to create a character who could pass as a modernized version of Batman, I - incorrectly, I think, in retrospect - landed on the idea of a "good" billionaire family.  And in trying to find good things for their huge company to do, it manages to reinforce a surprising amount of libertarian rhetoric, such as private institutions doing what government can't, and dismissing cozying up to authoritarian states as a means to eventually doing good, plus more than a few right-wing conspiracies, such as hinting that some people might have falsified their citizenship through some secretive left-wing funding.  I have written posts on my blog about how, whenever you make something, you never have the option of an "apolitical" statement.  You don't need to make your politics overt, but either you choose the politics with care, or society chooses them for you when you disinterestedly pick "defaults."  I chose too many defaults, even in an overtly political work.

This edition tries to tamp down the worst of those ideas, or at least tries to make them feel like errors in judgment and not solutions that we might aspire to.  Most prominently, I stepped the story back from looking like it endorsed new identities handed out by corporations, adjusting that to people in need of safety posing as League members who can go a while without anybody noticing that they seem to run around to multiple simultaneous locations, which seems less outright criminal.

I also tried to fix a couple of my other regrets with the book.  In particular, when introducing the protagonist's two old friends, I wanted to give enough of an impression of flirtiness that, if other writers wanted to continue the stories of these characters, they could spin one, the other, or both into a romance, but I also wanted to keep it subtle enough that I wouldn't offend anybody.  And...how can I put this?  Feh.  That sounds like a good way of putting it.  Who writes a story that allegorically mimics and points directly at an actual political division, calling one side bad, and then turns around to worry about offending the bad people somewhere else in the story?

The book still doesn't have or make space for actual relationships, and I didn't want to rewrite anybody's status between editions - recounting the same events - but I did at least make sure that explicit history and attraction gets a mention.  It has an openly bisexual, multi-racial protagonist, no longer someone who I have only granted you implied permission to interpret as such, if you don't mind.  And at least part of the book will now offend precisely the right people who might even hold their noses through the parts of punching discount-Nazis, which I consider a bonus.

Why bring this up at all?  Partly because this omission always bothered me.  But also, and maybe more important, it serves as a reminder that the Free Culture license means that I never *really* finish the book, and you can also do what you please with the characters or text.

Anyway.  "Dare to know," sure, but also dare to stand up for your principles, and use all your privilege and political capital - in person and on paper, primarily, not to already-full digital inboxes - to pressure governments and other organizations to stop this all from happening, now and again, and to speak the truth about what has happened.

 - John
