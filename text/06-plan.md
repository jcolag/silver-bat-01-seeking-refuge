# Lines and Businesses

> *Not like the brazen giant of Greek fame,*  
> *With conquering limbs astride from land to land;*  
> *Here at our sea-washed, sunset gates shall stand*  
> *A mighty woman with a torch, whose flame*  
> *Is the imprisoned lightning, and her name*  
> *Mother of Exiles. From her beacon-hand*  
> *Glows world-wide welcome; her mild eyes command*  
> *The air-bridged harbor that twin cities frame.*

> *"Keep, ancient lands, your storied pomp!" cries she*  
> *With silent lips. "Give me your tired, your poor,*  
> *Your huddled masses yearning to breathe free,*  
> *The wretched refuse of your teeming shore.*  
> *Send these, the homeless, tempest-tost to me,*  
> *I lift my lamp beside the golden door!*

* * *

Maps, rosters, schedules, inmate lists, blueprints, schematics, I have it all in front of me, thanks to Eadil, Fāng, and a bunch of code-breakers.  That's the good news.  The bad news?  I have it all in front of me, and my eyes have gone bleary from the information density.

As noted a few times in the financial press to praise their impending rise in stock price, Flint Consolidated Services Corporation owns and operates the Kinneson Operations Facility.  Most of the structure serves as a typical private prison:  It's bad, Flint almost serving as the embodiment of the school-to-prison pipeline in this part of the country, but it all happens in a repulsive legal way that we can better fight with elections and by demanding better candidates, as my grandfather pointed out.  If we can take the San Grobiano city council, then FCSC gets some well-needed oversight.  Granted, I'd rather kick someone in the groin and stop the exploitation today, but as Grandpa Robert explained, that might feel more cathartic in the moment, but it exposes and endangers enough of League operations that our story would end there, whereas oppression has grown much bigger than some minimum number of groins to kick.  We need systems that prevent the exploitation from happening, not a salve to every symptom while ignoring the disease.

That said, one wing of the complex gets far worse, the family-separation and kids-in-cages sort of place that has shown up in the news, their Refugee Resettlement Center that has no intention of resettling anybody.  That also all mostly has a legal patina, to everybody's chagrin.  Putting an end to it requires removing President Hepler from office, replacing his followers in the bureaucracy, and then digging a few layers deep in policy, since it's not like we've ever acted particularly kindly to immigrants if we didn't consider them "white" when they arrived.  While they mostly call people who look like me *passing*, Nana Isla and Mom still get "go back to where you came from" and far worse shouted at them, sometimes.  And, if a wealthy woman who beats up the forces of evil in her spare time can't find broad, persistent acceptance in the community, it seems hard for me to even imagine what someone like Jawara's mother probably deals with every day.

Interrupting that line of thought in order to head back in the general direction of a plan, as bad as Flint looks as a whole, in the back part of that refugee wing of Kinneson Operations Facility, we can find something even worse.  We started with theories, but a lot of this data provides proof that, there, agents from GORGON and the Clan of the Fiery Cross have started running their own refugee processing operation, smuggling vulnerable people in need across the border, stealing their identities, and putting them to work in a labor camp.  It's highly illegal, secret, and symbolic.  It seems like an ideal kind of target for us.  Except...

"Kinneson Air Force Base.  Highly classified and in the way of Flint's facility."

I nearly jump out of my skin at that comment.  And of *course* I spin around to find Nana Isla.  All the alarms at every possible entry, and disguised guards lurking out in the hall, and she still manages to get in without even a tiny disturbance.  I don't get it.

"How on Earth did you even - "  Actually, I can't even decide if I want the answer to that question, at least, not yet.  "Errr, I mean, what brings you to the great, historic city of San Grobiano?  Surely, you're not checking up on your favorite grandson."

"Don't get paranoid, Bertie.  Well, not about me, at least.  We had information on an assassin running around the suburbs, trying to take out progressive political candidates, best we can tell on behalf of the Dzyan Society.  A particularly strange one, too, that I'll have to tell you about, some day.  It must mean the money that we've invested in the races down here has started paying off, if they feel the need to these lengths to eliminate our preferred candidates.  Anyway, he won't do much of *that* work anymore."

"You didn't...?"

"Hm?  Oh, no, no killing.  A dead assassin can't warn his friends to steer clear of these fights.  He has quite a few bruises and probably a pretty bad concussion, but he safely reclines in police custody.  Well, safe until the Dzyan send another assassin to keep this clown from talking to the authorities, in all likelihood.  That wouldn't be my first choice when we could usefully interrogate the creep, and probably not his first choice, either.  Oh, and after all that, *then* I decided to check up on my favorite grandson, while I wait for my flight back home."

"Well, fair enough, I guess.  I could actually use some help in getting all this information on the Kinneson Operations Facility under some sort of control."

"I think that I can do that.  In fact, like I said when I made myself known, the first thing that you should notice sits here, the facility's proximity to Kinneson Air Force Base.  The entire place stands in the way of getting to Flint's monstrosity, but because the place has a high security classification, we're not going to get our hands on a map of that, so we'd need to go in without any clue of how to get through, but we might know someone..."

* * *

"I can't provide any specific information on anything inside the perimeter, or provide any abstract information that might allow the specifics to be inferred, because it really may be a matter of national security, though I cannot confirm that."  Major Merald Rubio Guerrero has become one of our operatives over the years and, fortunately for us, recently got a transfer to Kinneson Air Force Base, so he knows a thing or two about what we'll probably get into and managed to get far enough off the base for a couple of hours for a secure call.  "But I don't think it would put me too much of a pickle to provide information about the space immediately *outside* the perimeter, and arrange for the watch to look in a different direction for around fifteen minutes."

"At twenty miles across, that's going to cut things awfully tight."

"Definitely tight, but it should be doable, and it's also the amount of time that our surveillance resets take, including paperwork and coordination overhead.  Any more time would require sabotage, and that goes more than a few steps too far, with all due respect."

"Understood.  Can you think of any other caveats I should know about?"

"Well, our officers still have eyes and ears, and we can't really reset *them*, so the more inconspicuous you can be, the better off we'll all manage."

"So, eighty miles an hour, invisibly, without making a sound.  That could get interesting.  Major, thank you for your service, in uniform and out."  That may constitute a problem, actually, but we can't blame that on him.  The Silver Bat can certainly do it, traveling much faster and almost silently, but it only carries one person.  If we only needed one uncomfortable pilot to get through, then I could just bring it in from above.  "You'll also need another way out.  I could potentially find a way to re-cycle the security system, but like the entry, the timing would need us to pre-schedule it far enough off to not raise suspicions, and I assume you don't want to find yourselves trapped between military security and paramilitary attackers."

"Damn.  That...I should have thought of that."  I guess, if we still had literal drawing boards, I'd have to imagine bringing this plan back there.  "Hang on.  Maybe we've gone about this the wrong way.  We can make this work, but we'll need to take some time and...take a page out of Flint Consolidated's own playbook."

* * *

"What the Hell is all of this!?"  Admittedly, a bunch of soldiers with guns trained on me doesn't *exactly* match up to the way I wanted to run this operation, especially so early in the game, but this can still work.

"All of this, as you put it, is a pipeline to be.  There'll be some noise for a day or two, but we'll get out of your hair before ya know it."

"On whose authority?  This is government land!"

"Well, not anymore.  This being oil country, the government was highly motivated to unload a sliver of land to help executives sell more of that crude.  It's a Wooster project, I hear from the boys, so take it up with them."  I never thought I'd give thanks for the Hepler administration's cartoonish open corruption, let alone in the context of monopolizing natural resources, but we got here.  At least we went for land where nobody lives, I guess, and we don't actually plan to tear up the countryside.

"I will need to check this with my superiors.  We cannot - repeat, cannot - have your operations this close to classified activities.  None of this equipment has been cleared."

"You go do that, Colonel.  We have a pipeline to position before we start digging.  And have someone check out the equipment, while you're at it.  It's pipe.  Ain't nothin' going to be hidden inside except oil in a few weeks."  Well, I certainly didn't choose the best accent for this flimsy disguise.  With any luck, he has gotten too wrapped up in the indignation to notice how bizarre I sound.  And conveniently for our purposes, the excuse doesn't need to hold up for long.  A couple of hours more, and he'll never see this worker again, and this whole strip will just become yet one more derelict fossil fuel project in a world moving to cheaper renewable energy.

* * *

Ohhhh...we're going to die.  I thought flying the Silver Bat terrified me, but driving through an unconnected pipeline?  That feels so much worse than I could have even imagined.  And sound-proof the pipeline, and the roar of the engines and suddenly every tiny bump or skid echoes and cuts through even this fancy-pants protective head-gear.

The sound-proofing solves a couple of problems, though.  First, even though we can still move as fast as these things will go without causing a disaster, the pipes should sufficiently hide and muffle us that the Air Force won't throw any of their UFO-tracking technology - or whatever they actually test in there - our way.

Yikes!  Almost drifted up the pipe.  Falling would not only create a mess for (and of) me, but cause some serious damage to the people behind me, too, and scrub the mission.  Deep breaths.

Anyway...

Second benefit of the design, we point all this contained noise at Flint's people, and with the famously mysterious Air Force Base in full view, they should write off the sound as a flight test of some sort.  Major Guerrero has done some quiet surveillance, and reported that the guards rarely take notice of loud noise from this direction, whereas they tend to shoot first and shoot more later, if anything seems out of the ordinary from another direction.  Translation:  They think that they have the Air Force in their pocket.

As we get near a quarter of the way through, and I nearly ride up the side *again*, I realize that we probably should have rigged something with enough wheels to brace each of us near the center.  That would have made it easier to manage the dizziness than these bike-things.

Oh, well.  Live and learn, as they say.  Assuming we live, of course.  Living would make me so much happier.

Nearer to the halfway mark, it feels like an eternity, even though the calculations beforehand said that it would probably only take around five or six minutes at the most.  But I also have a sudden confidence that we'll get to the light at the end of the literal tunnel, possibly because I can actually see that light for the first time.  The rattling sound has lessened, which should mean that we've all gotten better at riding without shifting too far off the center-line, rollong along single file along the length of the pipe.  Readings suggest that the air has grown completely unbreathable, now, with all the exhaust pouring out.  To his credit, Guerrero caught that possibility and recommended oxygen masks; I'll have to owe him lunch for that insight.

Three-quarters, the knot in my stomach has come back as it sinks in how much we actually need to do in order to pull this stunt off, how many of those tasks require precision timing, and how thoroughly inexperienced I am, potentially taking this group into a fire-fight with members of actual evil secret conspiracies bent on destroying democracy and equality.  As a bonus, despite that little crisis of confidence, I manage to not wreck the vehicle.

As I come up on the last mile, less than a minute until all Hell breaks loose, I suddenly have plenty to do besides merely managing to not die.

Each of the vehicles has a radio jammer, activating one after the other to prevent the guards from coordinating easily.  Instead of receiving orders and status reports, they'll all spend the evening listening to **H.M.S. Pinafore**.

Then, seeders on the axles throw off a series of small explosive charges, set to detonate by proximity, unless they receive the coded signal, just as a small safety measure, to keep anybody from blocking our escape route.

Finally, a switch on my steering wheel launches a smoke grenade.  We'll already look pretty conspicuous screaming out of this pipe, so we might as well use that confusion to cover ourselves.  The explosion causes the pipe to shudder and shift over to the left, but I make it out.  I pull to the side, behind the pipe, to watch for four more of the team shoot out behind me.

The five radio jammers all launch into the air on small drones to land in positions separate from our own, giving the Flint crew more to do.  Out of the corner of my eye, I see one heading to trees, which should make it a bit more interesting to stop.  While that happens, I set up a strobe light to keep the guards off-balance.

Other drones, programmed to follow the smoke and then make for the trees, shoot non-lethal rubber shot.  It shouldn't cause anybody any real harm, but it should give the impression that we brought a significant force keeping the patrols busy.

To my dismay, off through the smoke, I hear real gunshots in the direction of the prison.  The guards must have already figured out this chaos didn't come out of an inconsequential Air Force experiment or a mundane pipeline.  We can't fight back directly, but a couple of concussion grenades should quiet them down a bit.

Either way, we're in it, now, past the point of no return, and the odds of pulling this off without hurting anybody has dropped dramatically and quickly.

And the guards keep coming.
