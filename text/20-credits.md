# Credits

As Isaac Newton put it in 1675, or at least nearly so, "if I have seen further it is by standing on the shoulders of Giants."  In this case, many elements from this story originate from other sources that have either fallen into the public domain - as of 2019, when I wrote the first edition - or the creators have made them available under a Free Culture license.

In many cases, these Giants allow us to populate our world in a way that looks credible, but allows for some emotional distance - and, let's get honest, probably some *legal* insulation, too - between the issues in the story and the issues in the real world.

As I think of this book as Free Culture and Open Source, I want to list the sources of the concepts, to make this work closer to reproducible.  That also means that the spelling and grammar checks will have an absolute fit for the next few thousand words.

## Plot

Bertie's overall personal story directly references and takes inspiration from Emma Orczy's **The Scarlet Pimpernel** (1903/1905) and its sequels, here about someone at the beginning of building a double life, instead of living one, and rescuing more vulnerable people than aristocrats in the aftermath of a democratic revolution.

The primary story of rescuing refugees references and somewhat exaggerates the global refugee crisis in general and the escalated crisis along the United States-Mexico border in particular.  For examples of news on the topic from contemporary sources available under Free Culture licenses - at least at the time; Common Dreams has changed their licenses in the intervening years, so you may want to check the Internet Archive for versions from 2019 - please see any of the following:

- <https://www.commondreams.org/views/2019/10/28/i-went-mexico-meet-asylum-seekers-trapped-border-what-i-saw> (Creative Commons Attribution-Share Alike 3.0 License)
- <https://www.commondreams.org/news/2019/10/14/doctors-demand-trump-close-inherently-immoral-immigrant-detention-centers-ahead-mass> (Creative Commons Attribution-Share Alike 3.0 License)
- <https://globalvoices.org/2016/04/23/how-ukraine-is-dealing-with-the-european-refugee-crisis/> (Creative Commons Attribution 3.0 Unported License)
- <https://en.wikinews.org/wiki/US_continues_to_deny_Red_Cross_access_to_all_detainees> (Creative Commons Attribution 2.5 License)
- <https://www.futurity.org/syria-united-states-refugees-1097812-2/> (Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 International License)
- <https://www.futurity.org/modern-slavery-is-widespread-for-refugees-in-england/> (Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 International License)

In short, I combined the worst aspects of these and similar stories for the version of the problem presented here, though largely attributed to shadowy secret societies, for which see below, rather than ordinary terrible humans.

Of course, as mentioned in the disclaimer, these don't really feel like issues likely to get fixed by even the most well-meaning of billionaires, but rather by all of us keeping pressure on politicians to do the right thing, and donating to support the work of real-world organizations such as the National Immigration Law Center, the ACLU, the Migration Policy Center, Alight, and quite a few others.

Charity Navigator has profiles and transparency ratings for many such non-profit organizations at <https://www.charitynavigator.org/index.cfm?bay=content.view&cpid=4665> as a starting point.  Charity Navigator does *not* make its data available under a Free Culture license, however, but it mostly works, as long as you don't buy into the silly idea that charities shouldn't spend money on overhead.

## Premise

Most prominently, the Wooster family and the valet Jeeves come from the early **Jeeves** stories by P. G. Wodehouse.  For the purposes of the world in which the League of the Silver Bat exists, the stories in the public domain as of 2019 in the United States provided a springboard for what I would consider canonical and include:

- **The Man with Two Left Feet** (1917), "Extricating Young Gussie"
- **My Man Jeeves** (1919)
- **Right Ho, Jeeves** (1922)
- **Bertie Changes His Mind** (1922)
- **The Inimitable Jeeves** (1923)

As of 2025, we could add **Carry On, Jeeves** (1925), with **Very Good, Jeeves** (1930) coming in the new year.  Many, but not all, of the chapter titles take their names, whether copied or derived, from titles from these sources.  The remaining chapter titles tend to reference the traditional names of steps in the so-called Hero's Journey, almost always in chapters subverting the idea.

In this world, Wodehouse's Bertie Wooster took inspiration for his secret life after a childhood trauma after the (real-world) stage presentation of **The Scarlet Pimpernel** in 1903 to create an international organization dedicated to rescuing oppressed people, especially from secret societies.  His "branding" derives largely from that created by Bruce Fordyce, the tragic bat-obsessed protagonist of **Number 87** (1922), by Eden Phillpotts under his "Harrington Hext" pseudonym, whose story our protagonist here partly recounts.

Wooster and the League of the Silver Bat began to have a presence in the United States during what amounts to an adaptation of **The Bat** by Mary Roberts Rinehart (1920, with film adaptations in the public domain in 1926 and 1959), where Bertie uses the Bat's notoriety to draw police attention to the fake Detective Anderson.  The silver-haired bat also, coincidentally, serves as a fixture across most of the United States, making the family's resettlement feel narratively appropriate.

Does the Silver Bat concept also take some inspiration from the success of a certain well-known bat-themed superhero character?  Maybe a little bit, at least in terms of his broad role, but most of the details that I might have borrowed from the idea evaporated as this world became better fleshed out.

## Locations

Real-world locations get referenced, on occasion, but the geography utilized comes from a patchwork of states and countries wedged in between the borders of what exists in our world.

Cibola and Quivira, the "Seven Cities," take a loose inspiration from:

- The legendary Seven Cities of Gold, supplying its naming;
- Places such as Hong Kong's *Kowloon* Walled City, Caracas's *Centro Financiero Confinanzas*, East Jerusalem's *Ras Khamis*, and Mumbai's *Dharavi*, providing the idea of a massive ad hoc urban area grown into almost an arcology housing millions, where sunlight has become rare, except for the elites; and
- Major California economic centers such as Silicon Valley and Hollywood, providing an economy that seems likely to have serious inequality, with billionaires frequently rubbing elbows with - and probably complaining about the existence of - the impoverished.

I've placed the Seven Cities in the fictional state of Saracinia, and it seems highly likely that the other five cities - Hawikuh, Halona, Kiakima, Kwakina, and Matsaki, in at least one source - got annexed, absorbed into the two bigger cities over time, and forgotten, even though the nickname for the area persists.

The state of Bensalem originates as an unknown sovereign nation in Francis Bacon's **New Atlantis** (1626), a quasi-utopia that, here, I have used as a state similar in role to Hawaii.

People once proposed South Jefferson as a new state that never got off the ground, its major city San Grobiano referring to Saint Grobian, described in *Das Narrenschiff* (1494) by satirist Sebastian Brant as the patron saint of vulgarity.  I imagine the city (and state) as feeling highly influenced by a hypothetical Esperanto-based culture somewhere near the United States, with Koralo Dareo International Airport - the given name referring to coral - trying to hint at that.

The freed refugees speak Esperanto as their native language, also suggesting a link to that South Jefferson cultural blending and that nearby culture.  In their case, they come from the Arceblanka Republic, which (by way of translation) refers to Maple White Land, the hidden plateau featured in **The Lost World** (1912) by Arthur Conan Doyle, perhaps suggesting that, in this world's history, Esperanto originated as a natural language in this region, though Esperantists may have also settled the plateau after the explorers identified it.

In sharp contrast to the Esperantists, the "guttural patois" of the GORGON agents borrows heavily from the vocabulary - sorry, *wordstock* - collected by retroactively protectionist versions of English, often called *Anglish*, which try to eliminate loan-words and build plausible substitutes for the loss.  This uses Old English versions of many words and more stereotypical Norse spellings when older forms don't exist, rather than working with a nonsensical mess of bogus diacritical marks as I have previously.

For example, by contrast, the first edition of this book used a broken and modified Danish with many diacritical marks added.  You can see it in the first edition, but it felt like a terrible idea on revision, for a handful of reasons.

- Using a foreign language made the white supremacy look comfortably foreign, rather than something that we've lived with in the United States since the first Europeans stumbled out here more than five centuries ago.
- Real people actually speak Danish, many almost certainly not part of any secret society bent on white supremacy.  And even despite the camouflage of altering spelling, it feels unfair to connect their culture with antagonists.
- Six years later, I find the majority of that text thoroughly incomprehensible, severely undercutting my theory of using the language for its similarity to English...though it does mean that I successfully avoided implicating any Danes.

By contrast, using "linguistically pure" English makes it feel more like something that could show up in our own backyards.  It matches the biological bigotry tonally to also reject "invasive" words.  And anybody who can already read the book can almost certainly parse out and sound out most of the alternate-English.  But for those who can't, I also list the relevant lines here, with my derivations and the original intent.

- *Lin-þí alsukj in þy stand* derives mechanically from *Lin-thee alsuch in thy stand*, and other than that mystery verb at the front, you can probably see the shape of "stop (yourself) right where you are," and I suspect that the context strongly suggests the meaning of the verb *lin*.
- *Þau lasð* derives from *Thou last*, probably the furthest from any literal meaning, but intended - by me, not history - as an idiomatic form of "listen up" or "I need you to listen," *last* in this sense referring to somebody following.
- *Ferg me* comes directly from *Ferk me*, a call for help, again with a less-familiar, obsolete verb.
- *Aheve þy fœlms, fœrsvy, œr við muð pull þí daun* derives from *Aheave thy folms, forswy, or wit mut pull thee down*, which once again, other than that noun, should visibly equate roughly to "put your hands up and shut up, or we (two of us) must kill you," with "pull down" seeming to have served - historically, this time - as such an idiom or euphemism.
- *Hvæð isð þis*, comes from *Hwæt ist this*, and probably takes the least effort to decipher to "What is this?"  One instance also used *þæt*/that, instead.
- *Hvæð kunnen ik døn fœr þí, miþíden*, derives from *Hwæt cunnen ic dōn for thee, mi-theeden*, which you can probably mostly get by sounding it out, as equivalent to "what can I do for you, (my) boss?"

If you compare with the original edition, their mock-Danish outbursts probably mean at least roughly the same things.  But as mentioned, misspelled Danish proved far more difficult for me to decipher than expected, other than a handful of words, so I can't guarantee perfect parity.  For those readers *actually* comparing editions, I have the least confidence that the first two lines match the intent of the broken-Danish versions originally spoken by the guards.

Here, GORGON - more about them, later - also enumerates things like the detention center cells using the names of runes to suggest a related obsession with Vikings or the Norse.

Javasu comes from the hoax crafted by Mary Willcocks Baker, also known as Princess Caraboo, in 1817.  Javasu served as the alleged homeland of the Princess.

Jolliginki names an African Kingdom in Hugh Lofting's **The Story of Doctor Dolittle** (1920).  Costaguana takes its name a South American republic in **Nostromo** (1904) by Joseph Conrad.  Djinnistan finds itself in danger in Karl May's **Ardistan und Dschinnistan** (1909).  *The Curious Republic of Gondour* (1919) by a posthumous Samuel Langhorne "Mark Twain" Clemens and **Graustark: The Story of a Love Behind a Throne** (1901) by George Barr McCutcheon introduce those countries.  Kravonia makes up the setting of **Sophy of Kravonia** (1905) and Ruritania the setting of **The Prisoner of Zenda** (1894), both by Anthony Hope.  Kukwana mildly adapts or modernizes Kukuanaland, a setting in H. Rider Haggard's **King Solomon's Mines** (1885), I hope suggesting some amount of decolonization.  Valderia presented itself as a South American dictatorship in **The Dreadnought of the Air** (1914) by Percy F. Westerman.  Tarshish (תַּרְשִׁישׁ) appears in the Tanakh (Ezekiel 23-27, among others) as a source of King Solomon's great wealth.

## Secret Societies

The book, at least in passing, makes use of many secret societies.  And that might require some explanation, before we get to the specifics.

.box {Does This Serve Any Purpose?} type:{note}
  In development of the ideas around the Luminary, prior to this book, I had the idea of creating a hidden/secret series of wars raging all the time, so that I might use them to decouple characters or ideas from fixed historical events.  For example, many early superheroes have their origins tied to World War II.  Using such a character in a story in a modern setting takes some awkwardness.  In comic books, they often shrug their shoulders and call the character immortal for some bespoke reason, or make the name a legacy, where the current holder happens to have *exactly* the same profile as the original.  On television, they might try to find some intermediate era to set the story when updating literary characters - usually the 1950s - or ignore the differences and try to modernize everything about the character.  In this case, the Underworld Wars provide a framework where events that *look* like a well-known war might have happened at any time, between gangs that have largely branded themselves around the theme of some existing or past nation regardless of their membership.
  
  However, it also nods a bit to how, if you take a step back, you can make a solid argument that every war for centuries has actually been part of an ongoing conflict that began with the so-called Second Hundred Years' War, the direct conflicts and spillover between Great Britain and France from (roughly) 1688's Nine Years' War to 1815 when the War of 1812 ended, and including conflicts such as the French and Indian Wars from 1688 to 1763.  Those wars sparked the Atlantic Revolutions from 1755 in Corsica to 1838 when the Upper and Lower Canada Rebellions ended in the creation of Canada as a sovereign nation.  In turn, that led to the end of many contemporary empires, with nearly every war since seeking to either continue or push back that anti-imperialist trend.

The Central Anarchist Council comes from **The Man Who Was Thursday** (1908) by G. K. Chesterton.

The Jade Moon Society, ASGARD, GORGON, and Laboratory 23 originate in **Bulletproof Blues** by Brandon Blackmoor and Sean Weir and licensed under the Creative Commons Attribution-Share Alike 4.0 License.

The Bird of Hermes Initiative and the Sentience comes out of the Fear Mythos, listed as an Open Source Organization, seemingly licensed under the Creative Commons Attribution-Share Alike License 3.0 Unported license.  Here, I have renamed the Sentience to Usikivu, a rough Swahili translation to provide a group that would fit into an African excursion.

ORIA (the Office for the Reclamation of Islamic Artifacts), the Three Moons Initiative (here presented as Tres Lunas), and Kinneson Air Force Base come from the **SCP Foundation**, made available under the Creative Commons Attribution-Share Alike 3.0 license.

The Palladists came from a hoax Satanist society reported on in 1891 by Léo Taxil (Gabriel Jogand-Pagès) and Adolphe Ricoux.  The Friends of the ABC served as the French revolutionaries in Victor Hugo's **Les Misérables** (1862).  The Clan of the Fiery Cross appeared in the **Superman** radio series from June 10th, 1946, to July 1st, 1946, treated as in the public domain as not having a "fixed" form that would have qualified for copyright at the time.  The Dzyan served as a fictional society discussed by Helena Blavatsky in **The Secret Doctrine** (1888)	and credited as the sources of the book.

**The Protocols of the Elders of Zion** (1903), you probably already know as a repulsive anti-Semitic hoax, here presented as a mass pluralistic conspiracy blaming Jewish people for their own manipulations.

The Illuminati really existed as a real-world secret society dedicated to the ideals of the Enlightenment and many other progressive causes, bringing them into conflict with most established powers at the time.  Beyond some Gothic fiction shortly after the society's decline, the idea of the Illuminati as a shadowy conspiracy manipulating world events seems to mostly arise after World War I in writings by Nesta Helen Webster and, rather predictably, has strong ties to anti-Semitic hate.

## People

Vito Delsante created Xavier Exley/*The Black Fist*, who licensed the character under a Creative Commons Attribution-Share Alike 3.0 Unported License.  The *Revenge of the Serpent Emperor* franchise comes from the Fictopedia community, where content comes available under a Creative Commons Attribution 3.0 Unported License.

President David "Dave" Hepler appears to have come from a 2008 hoax about - bizarre as this will sound - fictional Presidents of the United States, as in somebody created the entire story to smuggle into the article on fictional presidents.  As the hoax seems to have originated on Wikipedia, it comes available under the Creative Commons Attribution-Share Alike License.  The writers appear to have intended Hepler as some highly exaggerated parody of President George W. Bush, though presented as his successor.  I created his wife Ouida for this work, along with his background as a fraud-peddling "tech bro."  I make the assumption that he serves as the current President, rather than getting elected in 2008, but his imperial impulses have largely gotten curbed by the government and people around him.  (Note:  If a real-world David Hepler exists and feels mocked by this usage, I apologize for using and extending the characterization, but the character - a demagogue excited to put his name on everything and governing autocratically - proved too useful for the political situation in question.)

I based M͒ar̦kuen̻z S͖chmachenbùrg on *Cerebex*, appearing in Planet Comics #73 (Winter, 1953), where European dictator Schmachenburg had his brain implanted in a giant robot (Cerebex) that cut a path of destruction across Europe.  His given name originates here, and I have added bogus diacritical marks randomly to make the character less *overtly* German, at least.

Madame Strange, you can find as an early super-heroine who appeared in Great Comics #1 (November 1941), created by Charles A. "Chuck" Winter; the characters in this story assert that the character takes inspiration from a real-to-them Olga Mesmer, "The Girl with the X-Ray Eyes," a character drawn from her run in **Spicy Mystery Stories** from 1937 to 1938.  The Luminary had not appeared at the time of the first edition, but the modern person using the name made his debut in my 2023 short story *Bank on It* <https://john.colagioia.net/blog/2023/03/19/banks.html>, which also happens to involve Flint Consolidated Services Corporation, about which more later.

Note that most of the original characters - specifically the League agents and Bertie's friends - had their identities selected randomly across the world based on population centers, with many of their names selected using the no-longer-running UI Names project.  The names of the Arceblankan refugees come from randomly generated syllables, however, to isolate them from any real-world cultures.

## Companies

Brainz and NuloNetu take their names as fictionalized versions of real-world social networks running on open source software - which I will not name, even though many people who know the space can guess - that recruit users on freedom of speech grounds, and cultivate communities that tend towards obsession with right-wing conspiracy theories.  The Fediverse genuinely exists, a federated social network that uses the *ActivityPub* protocol and spreads across diverse software such as Friendica, Funkwhale, Mastodon, Next Cloud, Peer Tube, PixelFed, Pleroma, Write Freely, and other, mostly Free Software.  The platform has become substantially more popular since Twitter changed hands, but has mostly preserved its decent, inclusive community.

I adapted Flint Consolidated Services Corporation from ideas in **The Air Trust** (1915) by George Allan England.  In that novel, Isaac Flint does indeed attempt to create a monopoly on oxygen, so a modern equivalent of that trust seems appropriate to invest heavily in industries that cut against what consumers would prefer or need.

The Mammoth serves as a fancy midtown Manhattan hotel in **Alias the Night Wind** (1913) and its sequels by Frederick Van Rensselaer Dey using his "Varick Vanardy" pseudonym.  In this universe, they have presumably expanded, buying or building high-end hotels in other cities.

The names of most of the one-off online companies got adapted from output provided by Rico Sta. Cruz's Startup Name Generator, which dutifully throws all the then-available clichés at keywords.

## Miscellaneous

*Baritsu* named the fighting style preferred by Sherlock Holmes, in all likelihood a typo for or spoof of Bartitsu, the real-world self-defense art created by engineer Edward William Barton-Wright combining aspects of boxing, wrestling, fencing, savate, and other traditions.

*Bilphism* names a religion used, though not well described, in **The Beautiful and the Damned** (1922) by F. Scott Fitzgerald.

Poems used as epigrams include:

- *The New Colossus* (1833), Emma Lazarus, Chapter 6
- *Hearts in Exile* (1913), William Arthur Dunkerley, under the pseudonym John Oxenham, Chapter 9

I thought that some of this came off as mildly pretentious, and now think that it often comes off as highly pretentious, but they do fit the chapters in question, and maybe the whole story has at least some pretensions...

Less pretentious, parts of the book heavily reference and quote from Gilbert and Sullivan's **H.M.S. Pinafore**.

## Technical

I based the cover image on *An Alley in the City of Darkness* by Ian Lambot <https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:KWC_-_Alley.jpg>, made available under the Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 International license, and *dark, night, light* by Etienne Marais <https://stocksnap.io/photo/dark-night-LXCKQ8LU32>, made available under the Creative Commons Zero license.  The cover text uses the Ardeco font designed by Irina Chukina and made available under the terms of the OFL.  I assembled it all in Inkscape.

I set the interior text in Adobe's Source Sans Pro with headings in Comfortaa, designed by Johan Aakerlund and Cyreal, both also made available under the terms of the OFL.

For the layout, I took inspiration from mass-market paperbacks, envisioning this book as the sort of thing that I might have bought used for a quarter, back in the 1990s.

Finally, while I assembled the first edition of the book mostly manually - including a pass through LibreOffice Writer - I used Quarkdown for most of the layout in the second edition.  Using Quarkdown means that I had less control over page-breaks, but makes it more straightforward to create the final book.

## ...And Readers Like You

Somehow, I neglected this part in the first edition, but at this point, I pass the League of the Silver Bat to you.  I deliberately left questions open back in Saricinia, such as whether Wooster Amalgamated has an even deeper secret, or whether Eebee confides in - or has a romantic relationship with, sure - one of his friends.  Similarly, I tried to make every supporting character strong enough that (I hope) people could imagine them headlining their own stories, rather than making somebody like Yelena, Fāng, Eun-Ju, or Rajesh little more than an appendage to Eebee's story.  But the book also gestures at the vast world beyond, filled with agents of the League, plus many characters who have nothing to do with the League, though almost any of them could.

Some of these elements have clues scattered around the book for people to pick up, others provide a blank slate.  You have an entire world packed with secret societies and social movements, fictional governments and extreme corporations, superheroes and accountants.

You also have nearly four thousand words in this section explaining how I put this together, so you don't even have the excuse of now knowing how I managed it.  As I said in the original foreword, "the world and characters are now as much yours as mine, as long as you comply with the terms of the license."  If you play with my toys, you need to share with everybody else.

And I can't wait to see what you come up with.
