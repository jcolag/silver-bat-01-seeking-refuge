# Absent Treatment

Raj gets us back to the Wooster Amalgamated depot without incident, and I have to admit that I almost feel shocked at the lack of betrayal, ambush, or sudden disaster.  Workers at the depot split us up into much smaller groups, anywhere from three to eight in a batch, and parcel us out into an assortment of unmarked cars and vans from the fleet, each with a driver selected a couple of days ago.  Unlike the fiasco with someone beating Raj to us when we didn't know who to look for, the organization has fully identified and vetted these drivers.

Following the plan, we drive back to Quivira across a variety of routes to make us more difficult to track, while keeping in constant contact with Central, so that we don't risk yet another surprise attack.  It'll take another eight to twelve hours of driving, depending on conditions.  And it'll put a little more stress on everybody, but we can't easily get a hundred undocumented people through security and onto commercial flights, a charter flight would raise massive suspicions in this case, and everyone can finally get some sleep in the cars.

While I look over the map with some of the drivers, Hliang arrives in her truck shortly after we do, and I start to wish her luck, when she reveals that she will act as my driver once again.

Out of the corner of my eye, I see Cole moved into a security patrol car.  As I understand it, a doctor and psychiatrist will evaluate her, then someone will try to debrief her, and we'll hand her off to the San Grobiano District Attorney to talk about the crimes for which we can furnish actual proof.  If the city thinks that it seems worthwhile, I'll come back to testify to the broad strokes of our abduction, carefully talking around how we ended up stranded on the side of the road and willing to get into a stranger's tractor-trailer.  But that can all wait for another day.

Getting into the van assigned to us, we have a medic waiting, Dr. Alaba Olaleke.  She examines me first, not surprising, since I showed up with a makeshift cane, splints, and bandages.  Given the improvised treatment I've gotten and the punishment I've had to put my legs through in the last few hours, it seems so obvious as to make me consider skipping mention that the good doctor finds my current state objectionable in several ways.

She pulls out her medical kit and pumps me full of antibiotics and painkillers, then cleans and re-dresses my wounds, chiding me for not having done more to promote healing, as if I had much choice in the matter.

After Dr. Olaleke has finished harassing me about not rushing to a hospital in the middle of a heist and subsequent abduction, she suggests that I try to sleep, and moves on to check up on the other passengers to make sure that we haven't overlooked any problems or ignored any injuries.  Other than some bruises, exhaustion across the board, and a possible cold, the rest of them seem fine and, knowing that, I can finally think about getting some sleep.

Of course, sleep never comes this easy when I feel this wired.  I've stayed up for almost thirty hours and about half of that has involved a consistent fight-or-flight state, including getting knocked out by both injury and drugs.  But eventually, the monotony of the landscape and the exhaustion hit, lulling me into a real sleep for the first time in a while.

My dreams trend more towards on-the-nose than I'd ordinarily expect:  Running for extended periods without getting anywhere, feeling trapped while bad things happen around me, screaming when nobody can hear me, and falling forever with nothing to hold onto.  The usual, basically.  I can imagine a decent chance the screaming doesn't exclusively happen in a dream, which probably won't endear me much to the other passengers or Hliang.  Either way, I suddenly feel like somebody deprived me of a better imagination, with lazy dreams like this.

* * *

When I wake up, I can only see darkness outside, not surprising in the middle of the desert and well after dark.  Only tiny indicator lamps on the dashboard provide anything like detail inside the car.

"How are we getting along?"  I try to whisper, but I still feel groggy enough, and it has become quiet enough that I have no idea how successfully that goes.

"We've gotten across the Saracinia state border, just a couple of hours outside of Quivira," Hliang tells me.  "If we don't hit any disasters," c'mon, don't go and *jinx* it, "you might be able to sleep a few of hours in your own bed before breakfast.  And according to our transit communications, only one car stopped off at a Wooster Amalgamated-owned garage to help one of the passengers with some sort of panic attack."  She flicks a tablet over her shoulder.  It hits me square in the chest before I even realize that it left her hand.  "But everybody else is an hour or less in any direction from here, about to plan our final routes into the city and to the Arcology or safe-houses.  Even the delayed car is in constant contact to make sure that we don't lose track of them."

"Very nice work.  This makes the third time you've saved our lives."  I look at the screen to see the constellation of dots representing our cars and vans all heading generally towards the city limits.  Maybe I need to de-stress, but at this point, I almost feel let down by the fact that nobody has started shooting at us or trying to run us off the road.  I half-expected to wake up in chains on a cement floor, the way this mission has gone.

"It's all part of why we do this.  Think about how many people you saved just yesterday, lives changed.  Really think about that and let it sink in.  Then think about a few decades of it.  Just...try not to think about the pain and exhaustion, maybe.  Definitely don't think about needing to run from explosions."

"Explosions?  Seriously?"  I can't help laughing and hope that I don't wake anybody else.

"I mean, not every day, but it happens from time to time.  Things explode.  OK, mostly warehouses explode, for some reason.  Secret societies *love* to blow up warehouses.  Their insurance premiums must be the most worrisome thing in groups that mostly believe that science only exists to trick them into sometimes not acting like terrible humans."

"Well, regardless, thanks."

"If you fall back asleep, the best way you can thank me is to not scream so much, this time."

Well, that's a bit mortifying.  "Oh.  Sorry about that.  I was afraid those screams weren't just a part of my dream."

"I'm *mostly* just getting your goat about that, to be fair.  Your fit didn't last long, and we certainly all understand the day or two that you've had."

We continue talking for a while and, true to her word, Hliang drops me at my apartment complex around midnight.  I change and collapse into bed.  Eventually, I fall asleep again, but not before I watch every last one of our cars - minus the one that stopped off to calm the spiraling passenger that still has a bit more to do - arrive at the Arcology on the tablet, and then put together maybe half of a plan for tomorrow.  Or today, I guess, since we passed midnight a long time ago.

And this time, I sleep without dreaming, though I assume that I probably scream again a few times.
