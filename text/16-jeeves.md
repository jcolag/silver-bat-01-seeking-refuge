# The Inimitable Jeeves

The following morning, I call around the office to make sure an absent and extremely new Chief Executive Officer doesn't accidentally destroy the company's financial situation.  I probably shouldn't admit to any of this surprising me, but it looks like, after a hundred-plus years of corporate leadership by people who sometimes vanish for weeks at a time, the place more or less runs itself unless something big appears on the radar such as a merger.

Honestly, as I think about how well the place runs without a leader, I suspect that Wooster Amalgamated should probably run as something closer to a worker cooperative.  I float the idea by a few of the League-associated accountants and business folks, who quickly point out that various people have raised this possibility many times over the years and the sentiment always receives appreciation, but they consistently reject the plan, since privately held corporations run from the top-down make it infinitely easier to hide the money that goes into League operations and keep the company in its shady gray areas.  That certainly sounds like a rational-sounding assessment, even if it disappoints me.

I also check in with Wooster Amalgamated's staffing people.  As I assumed, Nick Liang did need a new job after realizing how Flint Consolidated runs its operation and getting reprimanded for not having sufficient dedication to...not get knocked unconscious, I guess?  Anyway, it turns out that he has a background in graphic design and teaching, so he'll start working out of one of our South Jefferson software subsidiaries working with our documentation teams and outreach programs.  I set a reminder in my calendar to check with him around this time next year.  I expect that I'll get in contact with him before then, but I want to talk to him then specifically to start bringing him into the League of the Silver Bat.  He does, after all, know how to handle himself in a pinch, and he has his heart in the right place.

Another phone call brings me into a discussion with our *other* staffing team about how the hundred or so refugees that my team rescued have come along.  They all arrived in the country trying to escape some serious trouble or other, of course, and a few of them have serious concern about their families, but it sounds like we have them otherwise moving along nicely.  I'll need to drop by and put in the time to get to know them a little better, maybe see how many of them I can put to work directly on projects.

For a few minutes, I look over statuses on various ongoing League operations, everywhere from Four Freedoms City back east to Javasu to Kukwana to Ruritania.  I recognize some names on the reports, with more than a few investigative journalists who have become famous for reporting around human rights in their parts of the world.  I glance at the lists of people rescued and the lists of people who we failed to rescue.  As always, it quickly becomes a lot to take in without retreating into cold analytics where the numbers matter more than the people, and I refuse to go down that path.

That *few minutes* rolls into hours, while I let myself get involved, which I only notice when Jeeves opens the curtains in my bedroom and drops off breakfast.  Yesterday took a lot out of me, I suspect, that any food would seem excellent, but returning to Jeeves's cooking after a week or so does my cold, dark heart some good, and so I come precariously close to swallowing the entire meal whole in excitement.

"How was your training, Jeeves."

"Excellent well, sir, I have learned mission coordination from a fishmonger."  I've found the biggest problem with the sort of classical education my parents forced on me means that I can't ever convince myself whether Jeeves speaks literally and happens to include a literary reference, or if the reference became the entire point of the situation.  In all fairness, I can't claim to stand above either one, myself, when talking to people.  Maybe my parents had that as a goal.  Yes, that sounds weird, but I don't know if it sounds any less weird than a family fighting a war against oppression and secret societies from the shadows, in all honesty.

"All right, so what do we have on the agenda for today?"  I finish breakfast and crawl out of bed to head for the main bathroom to clean myself up and start putting on the suit that Jeeves laid out for me.

"We have you scheduled to drop by the Arcology for some input on corporate acquisitions, and to file your report on last night's activities with your father.  After that, you will head off to a lunch with city business leaders."  That sounds like a good place to get my feet wet as the vapid rich kid role I'll need to inhabit out in the world.  "I then took the liberty of assuming that you would like to get at least somewhat involved with the processing of the people who you liberated from the GORGON camp, back at the Arcology, so I have left you a couple of hours there."  I can definitely to look forward to that.  "After that, you have an introductory Baritsu training session, given that your recent attempts to fight have proven rather conclusively that the two weeks you tried to become a boxer in college was, perhaps, insufficient to cover your propensity to mindless violence."

"Wow, way to hurt a guy!"

"Ahem.  My apologies to your ego, then, which apparently needs to learn to 'hurt a guy,' itself.  And then, later tonight, we have what may well be your greatest challenge in recent history, far more dangerous than your previous League missions and possibly more stressful for you."

That doesn't bode well at all.  "And what might that be, pray tell?"

"Miss Yelena's family has plans to throw her a surprise birthday party, which absolutely must include all of her friends, among whom, they list you, sir."

Damn.  He didn't exaggerate that one at all.  Since the last time I saw most of these people - Yelena as the most recent - I had a bogus stint at a drug rehab facility.  During that time, I had the goal of distancing myself from my friends, so that I could transform my image from that of a crusader out to save the world to a soulless and brainless corporate executive, in order to provide cover for League activities.

When it only involved me, it felt fine to let slip information about my secret clinics or to rant about rampant inequality enough that a clever person could connect it with what I do with my money and evenings, but those numbers add up much differently when I have responsibility for the secrecy of dozens of people actively putting their lives in danger at any given time.  It definitely won't make it easy to walk the line between seeming attentive enough to my friends to not feel like I've put on an act, but not so attentive that it seems likely that I would continue my side projects running secret mobile clinics and food banks.

Worse, my friends don't exactly fit the stereotypes of run-of-the-mill rich kids.  Waleed still works as the same investigative reporter as he did a few weeks ago, and Yelena investigates possible art forgeries at museums.  And so, a couple of hours of talking about coordinating my pocket squares and avoiding any topics relating to building a better world will just end up drawing attention, I bet.

"Jeeves, can I count on you to help guide me through the party?  You know most of these people almost as well as I do, and you have a good sense of what they need to believe about me."

"Indeed, sir, I feel confident that we will be able to craft a public persona for you that fills the varied requirements up to and including deceiving Miss Yelena, herself, without alienating her."

"When you put it like that, it doesn't sound like such a hot idea."

"And yet, it is quite necessary, for now, I assure you.  If anybody among the family or even acquaintances of your acquaintances has become initiated into any number of the seemingly unending supply of clandestine underworld societies, they might easily connect Wooster Amalgamated with the League of the Silver Bat, which would jeopardize every operation both now and in the future.  You might believe that you can trust them, and that would almost certainly hold true, but it grows increasingly unfair and dangerous to demand that they keep the League's secrets without first earning their knowing consent."

"You're right, of course.  But necessary tasks are not necessarily good tasks."

"As your father is frequently known to say, everything worth doing involves trade-offs of suboptimal solutions."

"True.  He gave me pretty much exactly that speech, encouraging me to get used to living with the consequences of this choice, and hoping that the choices were the best available at the time."

"And I'm sure your family has also informed you that, over time, you will have the capacity to bring people into the League to let them find a level of exposure and secrecy that they're comfortable with.  The walls that you begin erecting tonight do not need to be permanent."

"No, but what if I'm not able to tear the walls back down?"  I surprised myself a bit when I suddenly mist up at my own question, and I realize that I've started relying in many ways on Jeeves to serve as almost a surrogate father figure, when he's no older than me.  That feels incredibly unfair and will need to stop immediately...after I hear his advice.

"In my admittedly limited experience, there is no wall that cannot be torn down, so long as someone will set the effort and time against the task, whether those walls be physical, conceptual, or emotional."

"Thanks.  Really, thanks."

"I believe the relevant sentiment is 'all part of the service,' sir."  He either gives me false humility for effect or a deliberate move to remind me that he serves me as an employee rather than a friend.  I'll probably need to tease that out some other time.  Meantime, I have a meeting.

* * *

The acquisitions meeting runs pretty much exactly how I expect, a dozen fairly arbitrary-looking small companies that do reasonably interesting things that overlap with less-interesting things that Wooster Amalgamated does.  I have to suppress a laugh when I realize that, after decades of working with my father and grandfather in this position, the presentations about each of the companies mostly revolve around the corporate branding and pictures of their executives to make it all digestible to children with no attention span.

Jeeves briefed me about the companies beforehand, though, so can manage to seem to lean into this vapid appearance while making decisions that will make me comfortable.  One company builds infrastructure that they obviously intend for people to use to hide discrimination against customers, so the colors in its branding *really* "speaks to me."  In our hands, we can squash the initiative, plus use the patents to slow down similar projects as they come to market.  A company that plans to undercut our renewable energy products, their logo seems too boring for us to deal with, so we'll just need to live with licensing their technology or pouring money into competing with them fairly.  One company serves as a Laboratory 23 front, so we won't want their people or computers anywhere inside our firewall.  We go on like that for a while, until it gets near lunchtime.  I stand up.

"My apologies, gentlemen.  While you are all so especially charming and all of this debate about the usefulness of companies to the Wooster Amalgamated family is oh-so-entertaining, I have a prior engagement.  And so, to speed things along like a drunk on a highway, no, no, no, yes, no, yes, and yes, but only if they change that logo so it doesn't look *quite* so much like a...y'know."  I gesture, and it dawns on a few of them what I spotted in their design.  I know I only did it as a half-joke to emphasize the act of not caring about the details of the company, but it definitely won't win any prizes as a great logo and the team suddenly becomes obsessed with something that they somehow hadn't previously noticed.  "We can't have the Wooster name associated with a...let's call it a *marital aid*, especially not one so likely to chafe."

I get out of there before they can react further.  Jeeves whisks me over to a trendy Valderian restaurant uptown for lunch with the other neighborhood robber barons.  We manage to keep on schedule so far, so I can't resist making a couple of stops, first.

Now fashionably late, of course, I storm into the lunch like I've taken on a critical mission.  "Why hello, my fellow captains of industry!  Ooh, *this* one looks like a commodore of industry, n'est-ce pas?  Now, because I know - I *know* - this is such an important outing for us, I did some preparation ahead of time.  I brought everyone a fancy hat!  I knew your business suits would look so unspeakably drab, so please accept my contribution to the city economy."  This group has the bigger local employers, so plenty of software and movie executives.

I start pushing the hats on everybody's heads.  I stumble once with an "oh my stars and garters" exclamation as I slip a Flint Consolidated Services listening device under the table as I hear mutterings about how I somehow turned out "worse than the damned father."  Dad will definitely hear about *that* little tidbit, I can tell you that much.  We definitely don't see this as a competition, but if we *did* have a competition, I'd definitely have the better chance of winning the competition.

The meeting consists of a bunch of incriminating conversations as they look for buy-in on price-fixing, anti-poaching compacts, and blacklisting of labor organizers.  I pay particular attention to that list to make sure I get a copy of those names, since I'll want to plant most of them elsewhere and get them to train other organizers.  But mostly, I affect as much boredom as I can manage, double-checking my manicure and idly wondering aloud if my socks *really* match or if they merely come from the same batch of socks, and whether it's in fashion to wear socks at all, these days.

Keeping track of all their blatantly abusive business practices gets rough on occasion, but I keep asking them to get back in touch with me whenever they ask about Wooster Amalgamated's participation.  Helper's people in the trade commission probably won't do a whole lot, especially since most of this group donates to his fictional charities, but the Wooster family has quite a few friends in journalism that wouldn't mind a series of scoops of this magnitude.

I've had enough of this, though.  Even if I didn't already need to look bored, I can only deal with so much open discussion of criminal plans before I actually fall asleep, especially since I assume that Jeeves must still have our systems recording this over the radio to parcel out what I can use directly and what should go to publications.

"This has been an absolute blast, y'all, but I have to see a man about a horse, if you know what I mean, so I'm just going to toodle my way out.  But totally keep in touch, my new friends.  Call my receptionist or just pixel-at me.  Bye!"

I get in the car.  Jeeves turns from the driver's seat.  "How did it go, sir?"

"They think that I'm more vapid than Dad, and I can't imagine how vapid *they* need to be that they didn't realize I was playing them.  Hell, I half-convinced myself that the hat stunt would prod them to murder me outright, but no, they figured they were playing the rube.  Now that I've seen how utterly unobservant they are, I almost wish that I had the team stitch a listening device into each of the hats."

"That may have gone a step too far."

"I did say *almost*."

As we drive back to the Arcology, we discuss which parts of the discussion seem most useful for either League or Wooster Amalgamated leverage against their organizations, as opposed to the merely newsworthy or outright criminal.  By the time that I enter the offices again, I think we have a reasonably firm plan that I leave to Jeeves to expand on while I rush downstairs to what I assume will rate as the highlight of my day.

In the elevator, I throw my tie over my shoulder as if I've run here from across town, and it got away from me.  On the way through, as I encounter Wooster Amalgamated employees here and there, I run up to them as if I have a matter of life and death and ask them if they have an orange pen.  A few of them offer me a couple of pens and act like they don't understand me - frankly, I'd feel disappointed in myself if they *did* understand my goal - and act like they've condemned people to death.  "No.  No, no, no.  It needs to be *orange* or the project will be ruined!"  As I get further down into the building, I notice that anybody who doesn't wear a Silver Bat ring or pin steers clear of me, so it looks like word has already started spreading.

I could get used to this.  My mother probably didn't choose the wrong career.

When I walk into our shadow Human Resources department, our agents and the former refugees give me a standing ovation.  I shake some hands and ask what prompted all this.

"About half of us are impressed that your first big mission only ended with some heat exhaustion and a couple of flesh wounds, mostly your own."  The mere word reminds me that my leg still hurts a little bit, even after quite a bit of healing.  "The rest of us have just spent our time laughing at the texts our colleagues have started sending around to watch out for the weirdo boss and his mission to find an orange pen."

"What can I say.  Orange is an underrated color on sticky notes.  I was also admittedly banking on the vanishingly small probability that someone would actually have orange ink, since that'd end the trip right there."

As I get into the rhythm of things down here, both in talking to the former refugees for the first time in any real detail, and in handling some of the paperwork, I realize how smart it was for Jeeves to schedule this time in for me, and how Dad makes it seem so effortless to rattle off dozens of names of people he saved along with their cover stories.  I should ask Jeeves to schedule some time for me every day down here, when responsibilities have me in town.  It might not serve an important use to the process for me to physically show up, but I suspect the process will quickly become important to me, a constant reminder of why I keep getting stabbed in the foot and taking bullets.

Fojaist, who took my place pushing GORGON back with suppression fire a few weeks ago, worked as an architect, who we'll apparently start using up north after his new identity has gotten licensed.  Mnoqislasch *still* sings selections from **H.M.S. Pinafore**, making that recording an amazing investment, but it sounds like we'll try him out as a music teacher at one of our private schools, for now.  Beelul worked as a software developer, so we can probably use her right away; we have people at work looking to bring her family up from the Arceblanka Republic, since they may also need to worry about retribution from GORGON's agents.  Dunspri will head off to nursing school.  Ahagl, who carried me for hours and talked to me when I needed someone to talk to, he has asked to work in my office, and wants to get involved in League operations, so I'll have to find him something useful to do.  We'll need to investigate him, obviously, but the idea that someone would sit in a prison cell for weeks just in case someone staged a jailbreak with an organization that he can infiltrate...well, even in a span of time that has asked me to come to terms with a wide variety of far-fetched ideas, the idea that he secretly work as a double-agent seems cartoonishly far-fetched.  And honestly, if someone has gotten into this for that long a con, I want that person at my side even if they actually work against me, because that models a kind of dedication and talent that I can only aspire to.  Regardless of the details, it all has the upshot that most of them have at least the kernel of a plan for their next steps.

Many of them will, of course, also want to take intense English classes for a few weeks, so that they become less identifiable as *other* by the sorts of creeps who care, and just so they can function more easily.  At the same time as they talk about settling in on a new language, they invite me - well, more like *challenge* me - to join their Esperanto-only social media clique.  It doesn't surprise me that my fumbling attempts to speak anything other than English came across as from someone who needs a lot of help, and I feel grateful for that help and this new community.

As we wrap up processing the last of our group, I hear Jeeves buzzing around in my ear-piece, reminding me that I still have my first Baritsu lesson.  I try to make excuses, but apparently, this part of the schedule has become sacrosanct, since I did such a terrible job of solving problems with violence.  It has apparently become so important that Jeeves shows up personally to escort me to the dojo.

Down in the deep sub-basements, Jeeves and I walk into the dojo, where he introduces me to my instructor, Zhyparagul Kushbekov and sent into a small locker room to change into a loose-fitting robe.

When I return, in a vain attempt to show me what I should manage to pull off soon, she and Jeeves spar for a few minutes, and I quickly realize that Jeeves would make an excellent ringer, going toe to toe with Kushbekov for a solid five minutes before getting knocked flat on his back.  I have to admit that it impresses the heck out of me, all things considered.

Then, she motions for me to come over.

I assume that the repeated trouncings I receive, even when moving through the most basic sequences at quarter-speed, will not prove particularly interesting to most people beyond knowing that many of them occur, and they all cause substantial but brief pain.  I make that assumption, not *just* out of embarrassment - of which I also have plenty, make no mistake - but also because my head rattles for so long that I can't entirely recall what happened, with any degree of fidelity.  If my face hasn't completely bruised from all the punching and kicking, it'll surprise me more than anyone.

"So, is the pain some existential lesson that all fighting is inherently problematic, and I should always seek peaceful solutions when possible, leaving violence as a last resort?"  I've had to push myself a lot, today, so I freely admit that I may not have maintained the best of moods.

"Uhm...I mean, if that's what you *want* one of your takeaways for this session to be, more power to you, I guess.  But I actually think of it as more of a diagnostic session to get a sense of how you move, how that needs to change, and where we need to focus your training.  You're definitely not going to turn pro and rake in millions at fighting tournaments, but you're also not quite a lost cause."

"Maybe it's the concussion talking, but I'm not all that convinced that those words make sense."

"Sigh...All right, then.  Come at me again.  I'll work with your natural range of motion, instead of pushing back on it or exploiting the limits."

I do so, rushing her at full speed.  As I get close, she pulls on my shoulder and flips me over her shoulder like before, except this time, I land on my feet instead of my face.  I twist in order to face her, my arm swinging in her direction and it makes contact.  She counter-attacks and I take a foot in the chest, but I notice that my feet have gotten into in a position to sweep hers out from under her.  I do so, and Kushbekov finally falls to the ground.

"Now do the words make sense," she asks.  "I was almost as aggressive as when testing you, but the difference was acting as if my body was where your body expected it to be."

"That's absolutely amazing."

"Standard stuff.  You'll get the hang of it, starting tomorrow.  In the meantime, go hit the showers, kid.  You reek after all that."

She is not wrong.
