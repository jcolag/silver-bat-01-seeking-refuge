# The Metropolitan Touch

In most neighborhoods in Quivira, status equates to altitude.  There, the most manual laborers and retailers toil away at sea level and below, while the wealthy manage our investments while breathing the rarefied and much more open air of penthouses.

In parts of Cibola, however, that rule sometimes falls apart.  Mom used to tell me stories about meeting school-friends on rooftops in so-called bad neighborhoods, which I've come to assume had more to do with League activities than teenage parties.  She would say that she felt something around those neighborhoods where, even standing on top of the city, itself, with nothing but stars in sight in every direction, it still manages to feel at least as claustrophobic as a street with no sidewalk and only a sliver of the night sky visible directly above.

We start heading for the latter kind of place, no surprise there.  In these high-crime parts of town - street crime by desperate people, I mean, rather than white-collar crime that we also try to focus on more, but doesn't make as exciting a story - the upper levels of the city make an ideal setting, as drones and even makeshift airships make it easy to shift contraband and people around quietly.

On the way up, Dad walks up to a clothing kiosk that looks to mostly sell shawls and jackets.  He flashes some sort of hand signal to the owner and, when he nods, nods back and hands him a roll of cash.  Presumably, that payment prepares the way for our trip back down.

Looking down below us, I can see the entire spider-web of catwalks, miles of walkways crisscrossing and traversing the block, along with the occasional elevated road.  Because natural light doesn't penetrate far down from here, the area bustles with activity at all hours, with day often indistinguishable from night except by clock.

We approach the target by the highest catwalks, the operation setting up in the top floor of a building in the center.  We both wear what my father calls a Silver Bat "uniform," the contents of that secretive briefcase Dad handed me back at my apartment:  Dark blue tactical pants with mostly concealed pockets, a matching turtleneck shirt, a cap, and a dark reddish-brown fabric belt.  As we reach the entrance, I see the intent as my father unrolls the turtleneck up over his mouth and puts on tinted glasses and the cap:  It makes a simple mask.  I follow suit and also suddenly remember all the times while I grew up that one or the other of my parents showed up to an event wearing something similar but with a blazer on covering the turtleneck, making them look the part of an iconoclastic boardroom executive who cares more about fashion than the ins and outs of their own business.

It also occurs to me that, apart from the specialized pants, anybody could probably replace any element of this outfit at almost any retail outlet in the world.  That probably comes from intentional choices, as a way to prepare on-site instead of shipping unique clothing that somebody might recognize.

"Jeeves, we're here," he whispers into his headset.  My father's valet has served as the signal caller and support for my parents' missions for a couple of decades, now, with my man, also Jeeves, currently in training to support me when I go into the field, as if he doesn't do more than enough for me already, but this keeps our League activities in the family, so to speak.  "We can see our two targets.  Can you cut the power to this and the surrounding units?"

He holds up his hand in order to count down with is fingers.  Five, four, three, two, one...and the lights immediately go out, leaving us in darkness, except that the glasses that my father gave me seem to show light from the near-infrared, so I can still make out the shapes of our two thugs standing guard flailing around in the dark, albeit barely.

We burst in, each rushing straight for one the guards.  I knock mine against the wall, bringing home a series of jabs to his jaw with a crutch keeping me off my bad leg.  He tries to fight back, but unlike my previous fights, I have enough of an advantage that he can't do much.  He finally goes down.  I search him, relieving him of his weapons, a pistol and a baton.

Looking over at Dad, I see that he hasn't fallen far behind me.  He delivers a devastating round-house to his opponent that flattens him completely.  When he has finished taking the guard's weapons, he wheels around with a small flashlight in hand to show their would-be victims.  They all wear nothing more than rags and sit huddled together in terror.

"If you want to stay with these jackasses," Dad announces, "you can feel free to do what you please.  Everybody else, follow us out the door.  We'll get you home, resettle you, find you new homes, if you need."  The guard behind him stirs and my father kicks his foot back, smacking right into the man's head and knocking him back out.

A few seem hesitant or confused, but others translate for them, a language that I've never heard.  Soon enough, all of them seem ready to go.

"Jeeves," Dad gets back on his headset, "it looks like we have twenty, here.  Do we have Asmait available?  What about another driver nearer to our location?  Uh-huh.  Sure.  Look, nobody likes a smart-ass, Jeeves.  Can you get a driver down there with enough seats or do we need to take care of this locally?  Yep.  OK, that should work.  You probably should have led with that, honestly.  Fine."  I have never heard him speak to anybody like that, let alone the man who he needs to trust to dress him.

He addresses our little crowd, again.  "OK, we have a bus coming to take you to safety.  Come on, out here and down to the street."  He waves one after the other through the entrance.  "That's right, straight through and start making your way down."  He stops a woman, briefly, the woman who translated his words for the rest of the group, earlier.  "Dibgha, Avisha told us about your disappearance.  We should have you reunited with your wife by morning.  She should already be on a flight out this way."

She brightens considerably, given what everybody here has gone through, and shakes Dad's hand.  I can't make out the expression on her face, but if it looks anything like how I feel watching as little I can see in this darkness, it probably involves a fairly large smile and at least a couple of tears.

"Right.  Just keep heading down.  Five levels down, you'll see a kiosk where we've purchased jackets to keep you warm as you go.  When you reach the ground level, you should see a bus.  Your driver will be Asmait.  She'll take you someplace safe for the night, and we'll figure out the best way to get you somewhere permanent and safe in the morning.  If anything goes wrong, contact this number as soon as you can."  Dad hands out cards with a dedicated Wooster Amalgamated phone line, shaking each of their hands.

"You won't be coming with us," one asks.

"We think it would make you less safe for anyone to see you with us.  An escort will draw attention, while a more homogeneous group should pass through without anybody noticing you until you've gotten on your way."

He needs to explain himself a couple more times to convince them, but eventually they accept that they'll make it twenty floors down without our help or protection.

As they move out of sight, the lights turn back on, and he turns to me.

"I think that takes care of everything."  He kicks the thug again.  "And Jeeves tells me that the police should arrive shortly.  Shall we head out?"

"If we just want to head straight down, why wouldn't we watch over the victims?"

"I only bent the truth slightly when I mentioned that an escort would attract attention.  I didn't mention that it would specifically attract attention from the police, undermining our operation and making them seem too conspicuous."  In the distance, we can hear sirens.  "Speaking of which, now would be a good time to get moving."

"Sure."

I follow him out and up to the roof.  We cross to another street where the police haven't started congregating.  A quick glance shows that there we don't have many catwalks to work with, either.

"Do you trust me," Dad asks.

"I mean..."

He grabs my arm and jumps off the roof, pushing me slightly ahead of him.  We drop for what seems like an eternity, but probably only lasts four seconds, tops since much longer would end in a sudden deceleration smearing us across the pavement.  However long, those few second outright terrify me, with Dad pushing off from cables and pipes on our way down to reposition himself.  While he does that, I struggle to keep myself from screaming.  I can at least play at acting enough like an adult to accept that fear and screaming are normal parts of life, but noise doesn't seem like a great idea while on the run from both the police and organized crime.

Then, somewhere near halfway down, bursts of warm air seem to erupt from the street, shocking me, but more importantly slowing our fall to slightly less-dangerous speeds and triggering a small, high-tech parachute that my father apparently attached to my back when I focused my attention on our impending doom.  I have to give him the credit for a level of sneakiness that I didn't even know existed.

We drop another couple of floors until a large cable of some sort angles across the street and down.  My father has prepared for it and grabs on with his belt, and I follow suit, sliding our way a few blocks along as we finally reach the ground.  As we touch the ground, the cable vanishes.

On the street, we mingle with a crowd while we put our belts back on, roll down the turtlenecks on our shirts, and remove the hats and glasses.  Buying cheap sweaters at another clothing kiosk, we wander off with barely any evidence that the two mystery men, men who'll undoubtedly get reported as masked vigilantes in tomorrow's news, ever existed.

I raise a topic that makes me feel stupid.  "One thing I don't understand is where those air bursts came from.  I didn't see any industrial equipment near the ground that I'd expect something like that would take.  What gives?"

"Jeeves coordinated it, so you'll want to ask him for a more detailed explanation.  But my understanding is that it came from something like a very slow explosion shock wave, bolstered by what amounts to a handful of absurdly powerful leaf-blowers, though obviously any engineer would laugh at that kind of description.  He planted confederates throughout the neighborhood yesterday, so that I - we, once I asked you to join me - had a safe escape route available."

"So, that's what a mission looks like when you've planned everything out, eh?"

"Not quite.  I know that you overheard the transportation foul-up.  Thank goodness for Asmait's commitment to the work.  Speaking of which, Jeeves tells me that they all made it onto the bus safely and are en route to the safe-house, and the police have booked our two new friends who we left back in the apartment up there, their phones having a fair amount of evidence of their trafficking scheme.  If the District Attorney feels particularly ambitious this week, they might even use that evidence to pick up a few of their colleagues in this project."

"Well, I have to admit, that was impressive.  Thanks for bringing me in on it, even though you clearly didn't need me.  It felt good having something go off without a hitch, and it was surprisingly fun making it work mostly through brute force."
