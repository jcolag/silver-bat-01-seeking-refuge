# Bertie Learns the Ropes

I've seen a lot of relationships - I've been in a few, too - where you suddenly notice one *tiny* inconsistency, something that almost seems like it should amount to nothing consequential.  But you tug on it like a thread, because it seems out of place, and then your understanding of the entire relationship suddenly falls apart, bringing everything crashing down in the realization that your lover, your friend, your parent, your whatever isn't the person you thought they were.

Imagine that, but where the artifice of uncaring white-collar criminals falls, and you find out that they erected that artifice in the service of protecting the less fortunate, the exact thing I've spent my adult life trying to secretly build on a much smaller scale.  They do say that the apple doesn't fall far from the tree.

This morning has left me with little but a blur of introductions, meeting dozens of people just at Wooster Amalgamated's Saracinia headquarters who take on responsibilities for either keeping the company running or ensuring that the League operates safely.

The tone varies wildly as we move through the building.  All offices have those awful pictures of members of my family grinning for the camera while shaking hands with the likes of M͒ar̦kuen̻z S͖chmachenbùrg, Baron Ozama, Josef Stalin, or Laden Twala Twala.  But, in some of the less accessible rooms, dedicated to League business, each picture has a list of names next to it, people saved from labor camps or military raids, secreted out of the country often right under the smug despot's nose.  They also have a prominent list of those masses that the League has failed to save, from around the world, as many names as the League could track down.

And that doesn't even touch on the hour-long session with the scariest accountant who I've ever laid eyes on, who created a system of shell companies, political committees, and charities that make it easier for me to funnel my trust fund into feeding and healing disadvantaged people around Cibola...and apparently, providing treatment for my mother's leg, the other night.  It turns out that I've accidentally aided and abetted the liberation of local people in danger for the past few years, at least inside the Seven Cities, without any recognition flickering in my eyes.

When bringing this up, Mom brings me by the realty department, telling me about the neighborhood that she found herself using as an escape route, and how she wanted to pay a token of gratitude to the people who helped her, by lowering rents in the neighborhood.

I interrupt.  "Putting the complex under Wooster management only means they're beholden to whoever takes charge of the account, and it assumes we're in business indefinitely."

"Do you not trust those conditions after what you've seen, today?"

"People change.  The economy changes.  Nobody has power forever, which sounds like almost the entire point that Bertram wanted to make with the League.  What about creating something more like a community land trust, instead?  They get equity instead of a nominal discount, building their wealth instead of ours, and Wooster Amalgamated doesn't get to stand around like we saved everybody."

"We would still need to buy it to hand it over."

"True.  And then we give them some bogus song and dance about corporate mergers accidentally putting us in the landlord game - I have to imagine that the accountants can make that a reality - and how that creates far too much work for our lazy, corporate fat-cat butts, and so we have a strong motivation to help them take it off our hands at a small loss."

"Son, you learn fast."

"Apparently, from the best."

The whole thing as we walk back into the C-suite, hugs included, feels surreal, even dizzying, especially after a lifetime of assuming that my family existed to serve as the world's problem.  There, the family signs over enough of their shares to me to make me the majority shareholder, and then...I really should have seen this coming.

"Bertie, we have all had a long morning.  Go grab lunch to process all of this."  Mom gets that knowing grin on his face.  "Oh, and since you seemed so passionate, maybe even dramatic, about the apartment ownership issue, you should probably grab the paperwork from Julieta in Realty on your way out, and pitch your community land trust to them.  I assume that you'll want to see your idea through, after all.  You have an appointment to speak with the renters this afternoon."

Well, I walked right into that one.  Nana Isla surprises me again, seeming to appear out of nowhere on my way to the elevator.  Is my Nana a ninja?  Do I call her Nana because it sounds a little like ninja?  It actually spooks me a bit to ask a question like that, after the morning I've gone through.  "Your grandfather used to say that a critic is just someone who hasn't realized he's about to volunteer.  Words to live by."

"My son's first mission.  Aww."  Yep.  *I*'m definitely the dramatic one, Mom.

It occurs to me that I'm going to need backup on my - ahem - first mission.  Therefore, a quick stop between Realty and the front door.  "Eun-Ju?"  Meet Bhang Eun-Ju, the terrifying accountant from before.  She nods me over, lighting up as she pulls out a set of ledgers.

"Yes, Mr. Wooster.  I'm glad you're back.  I realized that we can set up an entire new layer of political committees to completely hide the money moving through them, as long as a few dollars occasionally do go to politicians who you want to support or have people see you as supporting, and you might also invest in a car dealership to move your - "

See?  Terrifying!  I sat through an hour of this, and I still sometimes need to count on my fingers.

"Thanks, but I will gratefully and lazily trust you on all that.  You've seen my spreadsheets, and it presumably proves that I don't know what I'm doing, financially, better than anything I can tell you.  I actually came by for your help on another project."

"Do tell..."

* * *

Mission accomplished!

We set up an arrangement loaning each of the residents some money, so they'll effectively still pay some "rent" for a while.  Three of them needed better jobs, and two needed childcare, which I think Wooster Amalgamated can handle.  Three more needed Eun-Ju's magic accountant pixie dust to keep the purchase off of the radar of abusive exes.  And we'll definitely to need to set them up with a good contractor willing to negotiate their rates, but the city has twenty-six new home-owners and their umbrella organization has a fiscal duty to keep prices affordable, should they expand in the future.

Eun-Ju stays behind to help a few of them with business plans, most legal, one or two...less so, but we can't exactly judge, since they won't really hurt anybody and we can't pretend that Eun-Ju's work doesn't already include an almost comical amount of money laundering.  Since she immediately started acting like a kid in an actual candy store, I slip out, but not before making a note to ask her if this sort of scheme would scale through the entire city, empowering hundreds of thousands instead of the dozens who we happen to notice on any given day.

"You look like the lady who fell from the sky and hurt her leg!"  Ah, this must be the kid who helped Mom, last night.

"I ask you to please pardon my Jawara, Mister Wooster.  He has spent the day telling stories about how he helped his *lady from the sky* when he really shouldn't have gone out in the first place."  His mother picks him up and holds Jawara close.

"Well, he sounds like quite the hero in training, patrolling the streets and saving people's lives.  I hope I can find someone around that brave when *I* fall from the sky, some day."  I wink at the kid when I realize that I probably started speaking past him.  The wink gets the laugh where the half-joke couldn't, and I quietly see myself out.

Down on one of the street levels, right as I start to get in my car, I hear an almost-familiar voice over my shoulder.

"Eebee?  Eebee Wooster?  Can that be you?"

"Yelena, it's been so long since we've seen each other!  How did you even make your way down to this neighborhood?  I know why I came to visit, but doesn't it run a little bit...beneath your station?"

"Of course not, silly, no more than it runs beneath yours.  My father may have re-married into money, but his sister's daughter's cousin's uncle's girlfriend lives up yonder."  A beat, then, "OK, my mother gave birth to me down here.  You've heard my father call me Marcia?  When he met my step-mom, he decided that sounded too low-class and tried to use my middle name, but we still have friends all through here."

"I'm sorry, I had no idea."  That feels like a gross understatement, but I don't have anything else to offer.

Yes, we dated, briefly.  No, it didn't work out.  No, it doesn't feel awkward.  Wait, maybe it feels awkward.  Where have I heard that before...?

"For a lot of my life, that lack of information became sort of the point, with my parents trying hard to hide where we came from.  But I don't see anything about it worth obsessing over anymore.  Y'know, I need to meet some folks down at the Summer Treasury museum for a small auxiliary art installation, sort of a private preview show.  You should come along, actually!  They'll have a lot of that industrial-look stuff you used to like, and the artist loves getting into grand scale arguments about economics.  It definitely has all the earmarks of your crowd."

I check the time.  I do still need some time to absorb this morning, and looking too anxious to get back to the office might seem suspicious.  "I think can do that, at least for a bit, for an old friend."

"Exceptional!  However, this event definitely...how can I put this?  This crowd will almost certainly *not* tolerate a three-piece business suit.  Let me take you shopping, Eebee."  Yelena's comment reminds me of Grandpa Robert's comment about affecting vanity to become invisible.  I can't say that I ever disagreed, given how often I dismissed him, and this seems like an excellent excuse to start.

"Then onward, fair maiden!"  I clumsily jump to the car door and wave her in.  "You speak true that I simply shan't be seen in this nightmare."  Heh.  Shan't.  OK, *now* I've gone down the dramatic path.  Thanks, Mom.  I'll probably need to dial it back.

Off we go for bespoke clothes, nevertheless.  Two grueling hours and change later and a lot of banter with Yelena and a series of tailors, and I don't think that I look *entirely* ridiculous or show my exhaustion, but I would definitely laugh at me if I saw me walking down the street.  And I suppose that, technically, describes the point of the exercise.

I briefly remember the passage in _The Scarlet Pimpernel_ describing Percy Blakeney, with his sleeves edged with,

 > ...billowy frills of finest Mechlin lace: the extravagantly short-waisted satin coat, wide-lapelled waistcoat, and tight-fitting striped breeches, set off his massive figure to perfection...

I haven't gone that far, but the goals?

 > ...one might have admired so fine a specimen of English manhood, until the foppish ways, the affected movements, the perpetual inane laugh, brought one's admiration of Sir Percy Blakeney to an abrupt close.

That hits a little bit close to home.

Yeah, I know.  Some kids grow up with stories about knights errant or youxia or whatnot rescuing rich people in distress in want of a spouse.  Some kids get picture books.  Instead of that, my family regaled me every night with the adventures of a rich weirdo creating a network of resistance fighters to save people from executions, a hero who acts like a total buffoon to hide in plain sight.  I'd argue that lands a bit too on-the-nose for what my family has hidden for generations, but more to my immediate point, I went through far too many years before I realized that not everybody could quote from the original novel.

Regardless of my exposure to the book, I hope that the family has gotten this part wrong.  I would much rather find some balance between the weighty responsibility I feel, now that I've seen behind the curtain at Wooster Amalgamated, and acting like a jackass to keep people from trusting me.  In the novel, after all, Marguerite waits almost too long before confiding her troubles to her complete flake of a husband, the novel's protagonist.  Who can afford to live like that?

Despite my distaste, I start to play the part.  As Yelena and I traverse the city, we stop off for manicures, pedicures, hairstyling, fragrances, and so forth.  I leave myself a note to send more business to that tailor, too, since this outfit looks like a nightmare, but still somehow manages to be comfortable.

"We might have hit the point of diminishing returns, Eebee," Yelena finally suggests, probably finally bored with this shaggy dog story of an outing.  "You look great, considering where we started, and the show showtime quickly approaches."

OK, fair.  I did forget that we went through all this to visit with Yelena's friend rather than my building a disguise.  Back in the car!

When we arrive at the museum, Yelena identifies us to the guard, who sends us to another door and apologizes that they don't have a private entrance.  We walk around and enter.  I see a handful of Wooster Amalgamated people milling around outside and then looking at some of the outer exhibits.  I guess that shouldn't surprise me much, since the Summer Treasury doesn't sit far from the office, and they traditionally do a good job finding exhibits that balance mainstream appeal with off-the-wall innovations.

A docent spots Yelena and, after looking my outfit over as if I just stepped out of a flying saucer, escorts us down a snaking corridor to what must come close to the rear of the building, if I have anything like a mental map of how we came around and through.  And when we reach the door...he vanishes.  I feel my heart rate increasing, suddenly concerned this will turn into some sort of scam as I open the door into absolute pitch blackness.

Yelena senses my hesitation and nudges me in.  "Oh, exceptional!  It looks like we made it in time for the unveiling."

I can't see or hear a thing.  Nor can I smell a thing, either, after the cologne Yelena picked out for me, so *that* bit of feigned vanity probably didn't make for the best plan.  I step carefully, as if I might randomly fall into some sort of tiger trap, while Yelena suddenly becomes cartoonishly confident, grabbing me by the elbow and marching me a few more dozen paces into the room.

And then, just like that, she vanishes, too.  If I still had a sense of smell, this would - 

"***Surprise***!!"  The lights come up and, when my vision clears, I see the entire exhibit hall packed with friends and family.  Nobody wished me a happy birthday, this morning, nobody gave me a moment to breathe, all to keep me from expecting *this*.  And I didn't expect it.  But, in all fairness, it feels like I've spent at least a week since I woke up, this morning, so I probably would have also felt less surprised to discover that I missed my birthday entirely.

I try to find something to say, something that lets me at least lie to myself that I don't come off as the sort of person touched and caught off-guard by something like a surprise party.  But I fail, and end up spending the better part of an hour gape-mouthed, hugging friends and family.

As I slowly manage to pull myself back together after an emotional day, Yelena admits that visiting her family made a perfect pretext to "accidentally" meet up with me.  She tells me all about them and the new land cooperative that they bought into.  From the sound of it, one of Eun-Ju's new clients is her cousin, and they want to repeat what we did with the apartment complex.

Grandma Serafina and Grandpa Robert pull me aside to compliment the new outfit, too.  Not that they can help providing notes in their back-handed compliments, and I will want to take them into account.  They also promise to help me find that balance, so that I don't need to exclusively live the life of a pathetic fop to get anything done.

The British cousins all showed, too, amazingly enough.  Some have thrown in with the family business.  Some have gotten involved with the secret underbelly of the family business, too.  I've only met a few of them before tonight, and most of them when we were just children, so I don't see any way I'll likely remember even a quarter of their names.

After an hour, like a lot of the guests, I find myself acting sloppily drunk, even though I noticed early on that the bartender started serving me non-alcoholic drinks early on.  When my mother passes by, I hang around her neck for show and ask her about the drinks.  Seeing other people approaching, she slurs out, "unfortunately, we have another errand to run, after the party is over."

I almost don't want to ask.

* * *

Ohhhh, crap.  Oh-crap-oh-crap-oh-crap.  This is how I'm going to d - 

No, get it together, kid!  Let them see weakness and they'll eat me alive.

"Bertie, even your wings out.  You don't want to stall at that speed," Mom explains over the radio.  "Also, your heart rate and galvanic skin response..."

"Aware of and working on both.  It just gets a *bit* stressful moving at this speed."  There, levelled off.  Now to fight down the panic.

No big deal, right?  It only seems that old Bertram Wooster's iconic inspiration turns out to have created a nuclear-powered jet with the literal face of a bat plastered across its nose.  This amateur scientist invented the thing about a century ago, then used it along with some sort of atomic rifle to assassinate a bunch of proto-fascists before he realized - when he planned to add the Kaiser to his list to end the First World War, only to see in the newspaper that Germany had already surrendered - that all the death and destruction probably made him the bad guy.  He took his own life, and left his estate to Bertram.

Bright side, I guess, not only did anti-science, anti-democracy Greenleaf P. Stubbs not get elected President of the United States because of the assassination campaign, but nobody really even remembers that people considered him extremely likely to sweep the 1920 election.  I only wish I could say that Stubbs served as the last or the most successful of that ilk...

C'mon, *focus*.

I can't give in to panic.  This monstrosity *only* has me hurtling along at hypersonic speeds in a coffin-sized box that doesn't look remotely aerodynamic, designed and built a century ago by a serial killer with a bat fetish.  It's just a normal day with the League of the Silver Bat, apparently.  And I guess that I should thank my lucky stars that our mad scientist Fordyce got excited by bats and not capybaras or dik-diks or whatever.  The League of the Porcelain Dik-Dik probably wouldn't have gained as much traction.

Honestly, if this didn't feel so blatantly dangerous, a person could learn to enjoy it.

Seriously, though, focus.  Hawaii below, less than half an hour from the mainland.  One wrong move at this speed would turn into a disaster, not only for me, and they've politely informed me that the techies haven't even figured out the top speed of this thing, or why it doesn't produce a sonic boom.

"Banking left, Control, heading for Bensalem."

"Roger, Silver Bat.  Notifying ground."  As I make the turn, even slowing down, I can feel the change in direction.  I have to wonder how this thing turns so smoothly.  At this speed, I'd expect to not so much "feel" anything as find myself thrown violently to the side fast enough to break most of my bones and pulp most of my internal organs.

The computer shows a landing platform ahead, inside a ring of some sort.  Oh, of course, I'll need to land this craft in a volcano.  Dormant, I hope, but even active would probably guarantee my safety better than flying this monster.  I activate the detection counter-measures and cut my speed.  As the island comes into view, yep, a volcano dead ahead, pardon the poor choice of words, and I flip on the automatic landing sequence, the metal wings starting to flap on a mostly vertical descent.

After touching down, I roll out of the bat while a middle-aged local mounts the platform and approaches.

"Welcome to Bensalem, Mr. Wooster.  You can call me Rangi, and it brings us great gladness that you could make your way here on such short notice."  Unsurprisingly, Rangi wears a silver bat ring.

"I only wish I had more information, but I didn't - "

"Nor would they tell you the full story.  We only pass details up the chain in an emergency, otherwise we invite the compromise of the network."  Rangi seems to have a way of taking the tension out of situations.  "By briefing agents on the details in person, we don't risk any third party intercepting descriptions of people in danger that they can exploit."

"Fair enough.  Let's get me briefed, then."

"In this case, the danger has its sights on a raft of refugees.  We had tried to coordinate with them to arrange for sanctuary while they make asylum claims.  However, a group who we believe takes its orders from the Jade Moon Society took a boat out to scuttle their raft, stranding our refugees clinging to a reef.  They have some time, but definitely not much."

"The aircraft doesn't have much passenger space.  Truth be told, it doesn't even have much pilot space."

"Indeed, we thought that this fantastical craft might be able to tow everybody to safety.  Once we have them out of immediate danger, we can coordinate an offensive to drive the Jade Moon out of state while lawyers file the asylum claims with Washington."

"Well, it seems worth a try, at any rate."

"Good.  We have a second raft ready to deploy with a lightweight tow cable."

Loading the new raft into a torpedo tube, I start the wing-flapping again to lift off and begin a search for the reef."

"Ground to Silver Bat," Rangi cuts in on the radio.  "We have a reading on our charges, on bearing three-two-two.  Infrared should detect them."  His plan bears out.  I see them clear as day against the water.

I circle the reef a couple of times, both to make sure that I can see how bad they have it, and so that they understand that I want to help rather than make their situation worse.  I come in low behind them to launch the raft, watching it expand a good mile away from anybody, so that it doesn't do more harm than good *and* gives me some space to make sure this towing scheme will work.

Touching down on the ocean's surface, I find a speed to drag the raft around and bring the raft to the reef.  When half the group climbs aboard the raft, so that we don't overload it and risk more lives, I slowly make my way to the shore.  This machine protects me at hypersonic speeds, but the tow cable, the raft, and the passengers probably wouldn't fare nearly as well at even sailfish speeds.

"Silver Bat to Ground," I tell the microphone, "the first batch will arrive in your hands shortly, and I'll set up for the next run immediately after."

Everything works fine until I return to the reef.  They have another boat, there.  Men with guns have started running across the reef towards our refugees.  Probably those Jade Mooners or whoever, out to finish their job.  I don't like that one bit.

However, I do have one small trick up my sleeve.  As I near the midpoint between the two groups, I set the wings to flapping and start up the nuclear engine.  The engine literally roars to life, scaring the living daylights out of these creeps.  Those who don't balk at the noise coming from a gigantic, silver bat certainly balk when I swing the raft into them and knock them a few hundred feet out into the ocean.

I tow the rest of the refugees into the Bensalem shore, tell Rangi about the attackers on the reef, and realize that, due to the magic of time zones and high speed travel, my birthday still hasn't ended, which makes this one extremely long day, in every sense of the word.  And with that realization comes one about me finally needing some sleep, soon, so I say my goodbyes to Rangi and fly home.

On the way back to the mainland, Mom corrects my flying technique a few times, surprisingly appreciated, given that I'd *still* just as soon not crash this monstrosity into the ocean.  Between near-death experiences, though, she fills me in on the long legal process of getting the refugees resettled and how we keep our part quiet, despite exposing the Silver Bat itself to the Jade Moon Society...whatever *that* might turn out to be.

All things considered, though, I'd call this rough work, but definitely work worth doing.  I do still have to admit, though, that I never thought that I'd spot my family as the people doing any that work.
