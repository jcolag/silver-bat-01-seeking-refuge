# Bertie Descends into the Underworld

Grandpa Robert keeps making the point that I saw him nearly every day of my life and completely dismissed him as a vapid dolt, happier for people to see him with tin-pot dictators than his own family.  "And if *you* thought that, Bertie, with years of talking to me almost every day, imagine how those self-absorbed despots saw me.  They all convinced themselves they found a patsy to give them legitimacy, even when their secret police came to them with evidence that I took a meeting with their rebels or stood around wherever they stash their political prisoners.  They always responded with something like *he's just a useful patsy under our thumb.  He must have gotten lost or decided to spread our message*, because as vain as I pretend at, and as much vanity as I might even have, warlords and fascists always have at least twice as much vanity."

"So, there's no way of maintaining anybody's respect, though?"

"I never could, at least not with anybody outside the League.  It has provided me much more utility to seem like the sort of person who won't surprise anybody if I flake out on a million-dollar meeting or seem perfectly willing to turn away from ethnic cleansing because a bigot has complimented my shoes.  It makes you invisible when you need it most.  We can't all move around as silently as Isla, after all."  He grins at that last part.

"But it seems cruel to carry that over into my personal life."

"I completely agree, but it will also increase the danger to your operations and to the people who you protect for others to see you in public as having strong convictions or a sense of responsibility, when you conspicuously refuse to display those same traits in front of the Board of Directors or some abusive government.  And it's also cruel to ask the people who you care about to take on your secret as their own, putting them in a position where they can treat you as respectable in private, but remember that they need to treat you as a complete dolt as soon as you cross a threshold into another room."

"I suppose, but what about - ?"

"Don't make the mistake of thinking about the League as some exclusive club, Bertie.  We often work with - hrm - 'independent contractors,' let's say, who we give glimpses into the bigger picture.  If they take the bait, if they have the willingness to continue cooperating once they've seen hints of what we do, we can bring them closer to our core operations until they become permanent parts of the organization.  Most of the brilliant people you met the other day started out running small errands or making some tiny, superficial update to a spreadsheet.  And you've seen your father when he walks into that isolated section of the accounting department or personnel; he shakes off the image of the vapid heir to a corrupt corporate throne, to become a respectable man of the people who I could never become.  I also would never want to go toe-to-toe with the version of your mother who walks in League spaces, and it makes me proud to work alongside her, even though the public hears my daughter-in-law and I throw insults at each other regularly in the tabloids, as if we're nothing more than squabbling children.  It doesn't make for an ideal life, but the League, in some ways, forms our community as well as our life's work."

"That provides *some* consolation, at least.  But how do I become this worthless pile of garbage?  I can't exactly call my friends to declare how, henceforth, I will act completely irresponsible and obnoxiously stupid.  I started acting the part while getting dressed for what turned out to be the surprise party, but that experience felt nauseating, and I don't even think Yelena really bought it."

"For better or worse, you get used to the nausea.  But the actual process, we have to leave up to you, since only you know your friends and your reputation.  The family story says that the Woosters already looked pretty awful in this respect, and so Bertram continued that act in public as he grew up and established the League in private.  My late mother claimed that she had the luxury of not having many close friends.  I used a death in the family to declare *carpe diem* to mine.  Your mother went into a rehabilitation facility for a faked drug overdose to distance herself from everybody.  Isla spent years undermining her own reputation and driving people away.  I won't lie.  It forms some of the toughest, most painful work that you'll ever do in your entire career.  However, as I mentioned, that pain will free you, later."

An addiction treatment facility might show the way to go, here.  It gives me an excuse to isolate myself and provides a reason to need to immerse myself in company politics and join up with the notional Evil Empire, in addition to siding with actual evil imperialists in the world.  Of course, to sell that idea, I'd need an abuse problem.  I'll text Jeeves later, asking for options that have a low probability of actually getting me addicted or killing me.  Death would really spoil the scam.

"Thanks, Grandpa.  At least I'll have support in this."

"You do.  All of us stand right here with you, whatever you ultimately decide.  Don't get any ideas about deaths in the family, though..."

* * *

I approach my father, worried about how this discussion might go.  They have pleasantly surprised me throughout the last couple of weeks, despite faking my way through a descent into substance abuse and trip to the treatment facility.  But as I think about things, I wonder if we've started missing the forest for the trees, somewhere.

"Dad, help me out, here.  A lot of our work - big picture - involves mitigating secondary and tertiary effects of inequality."

"In some respects, sure.  I don't know for sure that ethnic cleansing, for example, falls out of economic issues, but I can accept the premise."

"OK, and yet we operate a large, multinational corporation that gets most of its money through extractive means, perpetuating the inequality that causes problems."

"Well, yes and no."  Oh, no, I can already see that this will probably end in a headache at best and a furious argument at worst.  "Like a lot of things at Wooster Amalgamated, the reality depends on which sets of books you look at.  The public version of our finances does paint a picture of exploitative robber barons out to make a quick buck at every turn, and we do need to do a *little* bit of that to cover our tracks, unfortunately.  But a lot of our income actually comes from the patents on esoteric devices that we've seized, once our engineers have figured out how to eliminate their destructive power."

Oh, definitely a headache thing.  Oh, well, in for a penny, in for a pound, as they say.  And that sounds so weird, since we say that as people who don't use pounds as currency, not to mention the number of people trying to phase out pennies.  Anyway, my father has an expression on his face suggestion that I should go ahead and ask him to clarify his statement for me.  Why not?  "Devices?  What devices?"

"You haven't seen that end of business?  Interesting.  Well, you remember your mission to Bensalem, yes?  Rangi probably mentioned that the Jade Moon Society probably had a hand in the problem, and you let the reference slide past you to focus on saving lives.  Good call, incidentally.  Continue to always make that choice in the future; the people come first."  He issues a couple of commands to his computer and a chart of interconnected bubbles appears on the office's big screen.  "But Jade Moon represents only one of many secret societies around the world."

"Secret societies...like the League?"

"We have some similarities in the broadest concept, but as far as the term of art goes, the League operates as less of a society than...well, a *league*, just like the name says.  We organize an informal network of people cooperating on projects, almost all of our so-called agents paying forward help they've received from someone else, and only ever participating voluntarily.  By contrast, the likes of Jade Moon run along generational lines, often pseudo-religious with offers of revealing hidden secrets, helping members gain power, making elitist claims, demanding oaths of loyalty, and intent on either local or global governance."

Bubbles on the screen start lighting up with icons that we presumably use to identify members of each society, as Dad continues.  "Jade Moon, the Central Anarchists Council, Laboratory 23, the Palladists, the Bird of Hermes, the Illuminati, ASGARD, the Friends of the ABC, and so forth.  Some of these societies have lurked in the shadows for centuries, maybe even longer, if you believe their rhetoric; some are younger, many of the newer arising from rogue intelligence or security agencies that have figured out how to fund themselves after their governments officially shuttered their operations.  Some have well-known names, albeit usually as urban legends or fiction; some operate in significant obscurity.  They all rely primarily on violence and fight almost constantly over territory, similar to gang turf wars, sometimes spilling out into the public for brief periods in what we call the Underworld Wars."

"Underworld Wars?  You're pulling my leg."

"Nope, an inappropriately flippant term for a series of dire events.  The League, some of the societies, and even a handful of governments have actually used the term to describe it for at least half a century, some closer to a century.  It's not an accidental term, either, despite it obviously having tongue-in-cheek origins.  A lot of these societies have connections high up in governments, their original native governments, especially.  And as a result of those relationships, they can sometimes command entire armies by convincing leaders that their priorities overlap, such as Aegis starting out as a key operation between William Howard Taft's State and War Departments.  Therefore, certain small wars actually represent proxy wars between these secret societies, and so take on a much more global significance to us."

"Fair enough, but what does any of that have to do with Wooster Amalgamated?"

"Only *everything*, my boy.  A lot of the incidents we deal with come about as a result of underworld activity, whereas we tend to shy away from legitimate government activity unless things have gone too far for allied causes or us to try solving through political means."

"Not to interrupt," I half-mutter, "but put a pin in that, because I'll want to ask about so-called *legitimate government activity* that we seem happy to ignore."

"Indeed.  Where was I?  Ah, we deal with incidents that stem from underworld activity from secret societies.  Because as they try to - among other things - destroy each other as efficiently as possible, the societies put a *lot* of effort into research and development projects, often engineering technological marvels.  When we discover such technologies, we have the Wooster Amalgamated teams reverse engineer it, and then we commercialize it.  And that all gives you a *very* long-winded way to say that we actually manufacture and sell some useful things at a fair price, rather than making all our money from oil or eviction."

"Really?  For instance..."

"Well, the company's first big success came from Bertram selling what we'd now call televisions, as far back as 1915, and we had some primitive digital computer networks during World War II available, for organizations that the League believed in the work to which they would set their computers, which developed into ARPANET when we had finished with it.  The Avalon office has had a functioning quantum computer in the basement for about sixty years and rents time on it.  We managed to turn a few ray-guns into cooking devices or energy generators, too, preventing anyone from using them as weapons ever again."

"Whoa."  I can't believe such a pathetic non-comment slipped out of my mouth, but seriously, whoa.

"Yes.  In addition to that, we also have initiatives quietly replacing power grids with renewable energy sources, so the utility companies generally pay us for that while slowly reducing bills.  We have a few other revenue streams along similar lines.  It still doesn't come out to an ideal line of business, but as you point out, unfortunately, circumstances require that a certain amount of our income makes us look exactly like the sorts of people who would cozy up to dictators to see their golden toilets and jewel-encrusted limousines; without that, we could never play the big cons.  But you wanted to ask about government action, before.  Go ahead."

"Well, yes.  You've seen the same news I've seen.  Family separation.  Kids in literal cages.  Our own country has begun denying people asylum and imprisoning them in internment camps with substandard conditions, and the Hepler administration has only made the usual terrible situation worse and more explicitly cruel.  If the League makes its mission about preventing and rescuing people from oppression, we don't seem to do a very good job with the oppression happening in our name with our...well, OK, I assume the accountants make sure that we don't pay any actual tax dollars, because otherwise we'd look like we cared, but you get the idea."

"That's more than a fair criticism, Bertie, but represents a problem that grows quite a bit more complicated than sneaking into a facility under dark of night."

"Of *course* it is.  Enlighten me."

"Well, for one thing, we have serious limits to our power.  Taking on some cult or the security state in some banana republic is one thing, but taking on the security state of a global superpower is another thing entirely.  As much as I hate to admit it, sometimes might does indeed make right.  For another thing, while we have some separations, the fact of the matter is that Wooster Amalgamated and the League of the Silver Bat exist in a symbiotic relationship in many respects, and Wooster Amalgamated genuinely needs to do business here to survive.  For the League to confront the government risks exposure and destruction.  Additionally, policies in democracies should have their deliberation and decisions made by the people and their representatives, not some cabal of the wealthy.  We do our part on that end, advertising, lobbying, and funding journalism in the public interest, but interfering directly risks a slippery slope; as you yourself pointed out with respect to that apartment complex, people change, and it would be as naïve we we generally act, to trust even ourselves to maintain such high standards once we make a compromise that significant."

"Well, that's pretty disheartening."

"It really is.  We constantly try to navigate these waters, and try to influence policy in legitimate ways, but your analysis has it right, it feels terrible to not have the ability to protect every last person in need, especially when - as you say - they need someone to rescue them from activity done in our names by people claiming to represent us.  However, saving some still does far more good than saving nobody.  And even domestically, we have some options."

"Really?"  The headache has firmly set in, and I don't want to get my hopes up, here.

"Those secret societies that I brought up before, not to mention their individual agents, live and operate everywhere, including here in the states.  Agents of the Central Anarchists Council murdered Bertram's parents, for example, and the Council also had the power and responsibility behind the sweatshop that your mother liberated back before your birthday, depriving them of revenue as well as helping people in distress.  Jade Moon seems to center itself on the Korean peninsula, but they clearly have some agenda in Bensalem and around the world.  And then we can point to societies such as GORGON's local factions, and the Clan of the Fiery Cross, sometimes operating openly across the country, and that set themselves distinctly against refugees and with white supremacist ideals.  And we consider them all fair game, frankly, since any damage done to their organizations stays secret, since they can't exactly go reporting an illegal raid to the Bureau of Criminal Investigations, while they emphasize the need to protect their human trafficking or extortion rings."

Dad continues, as the bubbles on the screen multiply and spread out across a world map, little tentacles reaching into what looks like capital cities, if I remember enough geography tidbits.  "And that has its own effects on the process.  Since members of societies have infiltrated governments and push for policies that match their priorities, defanging one often causes the other to falter.  And similarly, as you can guess, GORGON and the Clan of the Fiery Cross both deliberately support Hepler and his policies, many of them posing as those non-violent rabid fans you see in the media eating in diners.  Laboratory 23 also seems to have pulled many of the levers on the voter suppression tactics we saw in the election."

"Wait, so when Hepler rants about his enemies in ORIA, the Elders of Zion, or Tres Lunas, those don't just come from the paranoid delusions of some creep in the throes of dementia, playing to the cheap seats on the Fediverse?"

"Some of the groups do exist, and they pose as much of a danger as any of the others, though not as the swarming armies that he claims on social media, nor are they as nation-centric or ethnocentric as Hepler's followers claim.  For example, a far cry from what **The Protocols of the Elders of Zion** claimed all those years ago, the Elders have no connection to Judaism, and quite a bit of evidence exists that they've gotten directly involved in ethnic cleansing against Jewish populations, and exposed their own name to gin up anti-Semitic sentiment.  In the same way, Hepler keeps naming them, and his allies keep trying to expose them, in hopes of forcing them to either provide support for his authoritarian vision or retreat from their strongholds in the country to stop competing with him, the usual corrupt bargains."

We go on about this for a while, but it finally sinks in that tech startup darling David L. Hepler comes from a family involved in GORGON, Fiery Cross, Laboratory 23, and others, himself.  His companies, from fruit-sharing platforms, to collaborative walking apps, to romance training simulations, to secondary coffin markets, all of those incredibly useless ideas gathered the venture capital that he used to get the attention of societies friendly to his garbage eugenicist philosophy for help in getting elected as President of the United States on the Folkhood Party ticket, with the Singularity Party also endorsing him instead of fronting one of their usual useless candidates.

Hepler won largely on technicalities, despite the Enlightenment candidate overwhelmingly supported by polls.  And while that victory will probably undergo investigations by political scientists and historians for centuries, the involvement of Laboratory 23 and similar groups probably means the evidence won't appear to conclusively prove anything.

However, as we talk out the connections between organizations, known members, and some of the operations that we know about, a pattern starts to form in my head.  And if we have a pattern, then we have an opportunity to disrupt the pattern.
