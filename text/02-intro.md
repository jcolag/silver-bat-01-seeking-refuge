# The Silver Bat

I woke up to five messages, this morning, two parents and three grandparents.  Did they text to wish me a happy birthday?  No, my family never works that way.  Instead, they all summoned me to the Quivira headquarters of Wooster Amalgamated, as if I scheduled an important meeting and flaked on them.  And "report for duty" doesn't sound like this is all for a surprise party.

And all five of them said that!

Wooster Amalgamated.  Yeah, that one.  You know the name.  The "you name it, we make it and sell it" company that my British ancestors founded.  The business that the founders' dim-witted descendants proudly brought to governments that consistently violate human rights.  The company that has offices in towns across the United States that you would never manage to find on a map, hiring people who nobody has met.  There's a decent chance that you've seen pictures of my father or grandfather shaking the hands of more than one despot or cutting the ribbon on an office building in the middle of some barren landscape.

I wouldn't necessarily say that I definitely come from a long line of criminal fascist creeps.  But I *would* definitely say that a lot of my family seems to enjoy standing in the shadows of fascists and around activities that criminals might also enjoy doing.

They also don't seem particularly bright.  My father has an hour blocked off on his schedule every morning to pick out a *wristwatch*.  Three assistants guide him through his day, and he still shows up late for everything, but the man will have a watch band that matches his socks, by God, or he will die trying!  He got the habit, and most of the watches, from his father, exactly like I have a closet full of watches that I only use when I need to kiss up to the family.

Granted, you could comfortably call me a waste, too, living off a trust fund, partying, and playing lacrosse when it isn't polo season, but I don't do any of it to make a buck off of oppressed minorities.  I do it to burn off some of the family fortune, to redistribute some wealth.  I do it, so that nobody notices the money that goes to soup kitchens and mobile clinics, which it turns out to barely even look like petty cash when you go through money like I do, and hiding it keeps Wooster Amalgamated from _helping_ by supplying equipment and supplies sourced from what I can only imagine would be the absolute best sweatshops and slave camps around the world.

Me?  I'm Egbert Buckminster Mannering Wooster.  My family calls me "Bertie," exactly like my father, grandfather, and so forth going back at least a hundred years, and I can't say for sure which name I hate more.  My friends just call me E. B. or Eebee, which at least sounds *slightly* less awkward.  For a few years at school, I campaigned for "Bucky," but nobody took my lead on that, even when I took jobs and introduced myself that way.  I should probably give thanks that I have more than enough money to not get stuck as a B. M. or an E. W., I guess.

Regardless, duty, whatever horrifying family duty this might be, calls.  I text Jeeves, but it turns out he already did his valet magic for the morning, setting out a double-breasted three-piece in precisely the shade of blue that makes grandpa angry.  Jeeves is an artist.  His family has been working for mine for generations, for some unknowable reason.  His father still works for my parents.  We bond over how stupidly evil my family is, sometimes, and he has gotten me out of more than a few scrapes.

Now, I *should* head directly to the Wooster Amalgamated headquarters, honestly, but I've gotten to feeling surly about the entire family ignoring my birthday to force me to turn into a corporate drone.  So, I plan to make a few stops, first.

* * *

I pull up to a hole-in-the-wall coffee shop on the Cibola border.  Most of the young Quiviran elites meet up in places like this, simply because it's usually a safe bet that our parents wouldn't let themselves be caught dead anywhere near the neighborhoods that host them.

"Waleed, it's been too long!"  We hug.  Waleed Sadozai became one of my close friends in college.  A sharp, up-and-coming investigative journalist with a few published historical fiction novels on the side, I connected with him as the kind of guy who I can rant with about the cognitive dissonance between living with basically unlimited wealth while hustling quietly to fix inequality.  I do it as barely a dilettante, but he deliberately made it his career.

Yes, we dated, briefly.  No, it didn't work out.  No, it doesn't feel awkward.  Wait, maybe it feels awkward.

"It has, man.  I'm sorry I didn't swing by when your grandfather passed," he says, breaking me out of my attempt to overthink things.

"No worries.  I knew you went off doing good things, and honestly, reading your articles on the uprisings in Tarshish did a lot to help me get through the grief.  Sounds like they still have a long way to go, there, but am I right in saying that their new unions actually push back on working for the Tarshish war machine or supporting any harmful policies?"

"Wow, you *did* read them!  I don't know if even my editor got that deep into it.  Yeah, I found this amazing outpouring of frustration with a government completely out of control and, while President Sha'ban can stay in power, the majority of the country stands in solidarity and refuses to provide support for his policies, and that has forced his administration to mostly behave.  We can't get near enough to their concentration camps to get confirmation, but it sounds like they may have even started releasing the Kirii in small numbers."

"Completely amazing, and a nightmare that it doesn't happen in *other* places."  I gesture to a rack of newspapers with headlines trying to dance around saying that certain border patrols have started ripping children from their families and either imprisoning the children or leaving them with foster families with no paperwork, like an industrial-scale kidnapping scheme.  "A work stoppage in protest wouldn't exactly cause the world to stop spinning, or even reducing shopping, and I wish that someone would have suggested something like that under the previous administration, one that didn't want to jump completely off the rails."

"Believe me, I know, never stop fighting and always vote for who you want to fight.  But I can barely convince my own colleagues that we shouldn't let our employers pay people artificially low wages and then sell out to hedge funds intent on gutting the entire operation.  A national work stoppage seems out of the question, when even the people who would report on such a movement don't feel convinced that cooperative effort has a good enough return on investment.  Sorry to be a downer, though.  How have your...side projects shaped up?"

"Not bad, actually.  Your girlfriend put me in touch with a few companies looking to get cheap food and meds off the market, which I'm all too happy to help them out with.  And one of the kids who works with me on the clinics came up with a design for the cars that not only look appealing, but distinctive enough that we can spread the word and have people who need us find us while also looking generic enough that we can quickly camouflage them and merge into traffic if any authorities get suspicious that we might have aided and abetted or something.  We've also started the earliest stages of maybe looking at putting some effort towards housing issues."

"That sounds amazing.  I wish I could put my trust fund to better use."

"Stupid question, but have you considered buying your employers out?  Or smaller outlets that are struggling?  It would give you more flexibility and, since I know you don't want to become *management*, you could have a lawyer wave their magic wand and turn those papers and podcasts into worker cooperatives, solving your unionization problem at the same time."

His eyes suddenly widen.  "I really want to say that I've thought about doing exactly that, but no, I feel like an ostrich, head in the sand, who's had too little time after throwing an extended pity party for myself instead of looking at my problems like an outsider.  I blame television."

"Seriously, man?  We're far too young to blame television, and our parents love jumping on the bleeding edge of the technology curve so much that I actually don't know how either of us has ever even *seen* a television.  We should probably blame social media or voice assistants or something."

"Fair enough.  I'll blame the white supremacists and sexists quietly lurking on Brainz and NuloNetu."

"Dude, they do *not* lurk.  Something like seven out of every ten posts comes packed to the gills with some wild conspiracy theory about the country 'collapsing' because we don't have vigilante mobs roaming the streets to murder uppity minorities anymore.  And something like another two out of every ten comes from someone's broken bot that serves up word salad."

"Funny you should mention that, actually.  I have colleagues who started investigating moderation on social media sites.  Every time that I talk to them, it feels like someone *just* told them something that took years off their lives.  Watch out for those articles, but take care going in, because they will not make many readers happy."

"I really want to say that I can't wait, but you're right, I really have no excuse for the way that things have gone, and no explanation can ease the pain of someone whose home address got aired in public by some jerk, so that certain people will attack it."

"Yeah."  His phone makes some sort sound that's of cross between a beep and...I guess a fart, maybe, and I feel extremely uneasy at how unsure I feel about how funny it is.  Fortunately, Waleed interrupts my confusion.  "I'm so sorry, Eebee.  That was from my editor.  Something significant is going on in Gondour.  They have the potential to push out another tin-pot dictator wannabe.  I'll think about what you said, though, man, because that might be the solution."

"Seriously, no worries."  I give him another hug.  "Go save the world by exposing corruption and lifting up heroes, man.  Go save us."  He gives the barista a huge tip and leaves.

All right, that was fun, but *now* the time has come to follow Waleed's example and head off to the Arcology!

* * *

Wooster Amalgamated's American headquarters, called "the Arcology," lies at the heart of downtown Quivira.

If you haven't come out this way, from a distance, the entirety of the Seven Cities (as a side note, not really seven) comes close to resembling a single building.  One building blends into the next, sometimes constructed on top of each other or over and under roads, with sky-ways and suspended walkways creating an environment where a person could live an entire life without touching the ground or seeing the Sun, and quite a few of our poorer residents do exactly that, unfortunately.

The Arcology takes that premise a step further and glamorizes it.  Downtown comprises several buildings that stand discretely apart from the rest of the city above-ground, where zoning laws give capital a tiny bit of elbow room, and among those buildings, you can find the behemoth eyesore architected by my great-grandfather.  Inside Wooster Amalgamated's headquarters, they have a complete ecosystem with air and water recycling and some indoor farming, plus facilities where the thousands of employees can move in, if they so desired.  As far as I know, no actual employee has taken them up on the offer, of course, except during the occasional divorce or eviction, so the housing mostly serves as a luxury hotel for corporate visitors.

Typical Wooster folly, thinking that workers would want to live at their offices and shop at the company store.  We have found the embodiment of late-stage capitalism at its finest.

The family's offices sit in the penthouse, the better to look down on everyone else, I'll bet.  The glass elevator rising through a mini-jungle looks pretty, at least.

On the higher floors, I start to notice a surprising number of people wear similar jewelry, silver rings, pins, and brooches that look like an upside-down A with the crossbar extended into a kind of frown, nothing flashy, but when person after person wears it, it looks conspicuous.  These people have one weird rich-folks fad, and it probably feels weirder that I haven't gotten in on it.  Must be someone's "follow your dreams" kid selling them or something.

Dad's assistant Nisa, one of those strange pins on her lapel, ushers me into the office.

"Bertie, my lad, take a seat.  Oh, maybe not *that* seat.  The sun only draws attention to that travesty of a blue."  What would I do without you, Jeeves?

"Father, knock it off.  We came here this morning to reveal secrets, not perpetuate them."  Dad clicks a remote control and the screens behind the family light up.  The screens show...uh-oh, the all show images from the soup kitchens and clinics that I back.  They *know*.

"Bertie, relax," Mom tries to soothe as I panic.  "We've known about these side projects all along, and we want to help."  I'll bet.  Help use them to funnel opioids directly to kids, so that their pharmaceutical partners don't need to spend so much time marketing that crap?  Help exploit them for cheap labor?

"I'm sorry, everyone, but this all draws from money you've already given me, and I have no interest in Wooster Amalgamated oversight or handshakes with war criminals."

"You misunderstand, son."  Another click changes the screens.  Sweatshops.  Illegal detention camps.  Torture sites.

"If you're threatening me..."

"What?  Ha!  Oh, no, keep watching.  The bottom left is my favorite of them, if you're not sure where to direct your attention."  I have to admit, this still sounds like a threat.

Then, shocking me in how abrupt the transition comes, in each of the terrible scenes - synchronized by my father's editing, no doubt - someone bursts in.  They represent a wide variety of genders and ethnicities.  Some wear masks, others wear ordinary street clothes with parts of their face strategically covered.  Some physically fight the abusive authorities in charge, some tranquilize them from the door, one even bribes them.  But each of these interlopers protects the people in trouble and guides them out before each screen goes black.

And to every last person, they all wear that same jewelry.  My parents do, too, I see now that I've started looking for it.  The same with my grandparents.  Something doesn't seem to connect up, here.  I guess I might as well ask.

"OK, so what, Wooster Amalgamated produces low-budget action movies, now, and you want to use my network to find your next star?"

"Oh, you've grown Bertie, so cynical for such an idealistic rebel."  Grandma Serafina knows how to hurt a guy.  "Third screen from the left, what do you see in the background?"  The videos either restart or change to a new batch.

This scene features a woman with a bandana over her face.  A cartel agent tries to sneak up and stab her, but she elbows him at the price of a flesh wound, knocking him over with a boot on his neck.  While I watch the loop, Grandma rolls up her sleeve to reveal the scar, matching the gash on the arm.

"So, we secretly manage a family of superheroes posing as vulture capitalists that teams up with Madame Strange on the weekends to fight alien invasions?"

"Don't act so dramatic, Bertie."  Mom, the former actress, telling _me_ not to get dramatic.  "For one thing, Madame Strange was only a role that Olga Mesmer created for the Alectrion serials, because she couldn't get anybody to take her seriously in the field and the fiction got the attention away from her."

"My father claimed he did help the Luminary out a couple of times, though."  Grandpa Robert somehow managed to survive this family with a reasonable "Bert" name.

"Grandfather," I say, "the Luminary was just some kind of urban legend.  Strange visitor from another - "

"He retired decades ago, and he helps fund marketing that keeps Madame Strange productions in the public eye to misdirect everybody, but he is real enough that you have met him once or twice.  I won't tell you who, though.  I have to leave it for him to tell, some day, when he sees fit."  A likely story.  Unless...

"That's also not the point of this meeting, Father.  Bertie, you have the details wrong, and we certainly wouldn't call ourselves heroes, only normal people who try to do our parts in the world, along with a lot of help."

"Oh, I see.  You want to tell me that Wooster Amalgamated *really* serves as a secret front for some sort of expensive Scarlet Pimpernel?  Sure.  I'll be going now, maybe find some not-lying people to talk to for a while.  And maybe the superheroes you claim secretly exist can provide the entertainment at the next Solstice party."

"Bertie, let's start over, because you've landed quite close to the mark, and I think that we're looking in the same direction.  Follow, and remember who used to read you *The Scarlet Pimpernel* when you were young."  He gestures out, and we all get into the elevator as I get a pit in my stomach, like I'm about to get a theatrical *we're not so different, you and I* speech in a stage whisper.  "You know, and have probably come to hate, this painting of Bertram Wooster."

"The man who expanded Wooster Amalgamated and started collaborations with war criminals?  Yeah, you might say that pretty much covers my feelings."

"What we generally don't put in the biographies is that he lost his parents young, murdered by an anarchist because of their aristocratic backgrounds."

"Good for the anarchist," I mutter.  I know that I'm not improving things and yet, somehow, Dad isn't taking the bait.

"Heh.  Well, the murder happened as they left one of the few stagings of *The Scarlet Pimpernel*.  The play stuck with him and inspired him to live his life defending people under attack for their identities."

"What, hence palling around with war criminals?"

"Bertie, my sweet child," Grandma Serafina interrupts, "recall Poe's *Purloined Letter*, where the detective finds what the prefect cannot, because it lies right out in the open, while officials search out more elaborate hiding places."

"And you'll learn quickly that the merest display of vanity," Grandpa Robert sniffs as he strokes his eyebrow, "a few well-timed malapropisms, and the occasional gallingly conspicuous disregard for human rights in front of the right people, and like the Pimpernel, they seek you here, and they'll seek you there, never considering the damned, elusive agent has spent the last twenty minutes posing with them for a photo op.  And for all the negotiations, and all that blood-money thrown at projects, somehow our wildly successful corporation never manages to deliver for these terrible regimes due to so many improbable obstacles, or what they take possession of often has unpredictable gaps in security."

"No..."

"You already know a little something about that, yourself, given the number of sports cars you seem to lose in funding your own enterprises."  Without any warning, I've found myself in the middle of an argument and losing badly, without any idea of how I got here.  Of all the times to have the realization that I might have become exactly like my parents...

"And that brings us back to Bertram, who tried but failed to do anything about the Thracian genocide.  He later hired a team to join him in Armenia, only saving a few dozen lives, but he knew that he had hit onto something.  His underground railroad in the face of the October Revolution was a bit more successful, but a few years later that, with a small inheritance of technology from a former drinking buddy, he came on the specific plan for the League of the Silver Bat, a resistance organization modeled loosely after his role-models in the League of the Scarlet Pimpernel."  I can't think of a single way that he could have successfully timed that speech to end precisely as the elevator doors re-open, but here we are.

"But we can't always meddle about with large-scale, capital-O oppression," Dad continues.  "Often, we can't get anywhere near it, because those cases have become too high profile, grown too well-defended.  Vigilantism, exploitation, domestic abuse, terrorism, even abuse of power, each individual in danger makes a difference and, yes, sometimes that means cozying up to some spectacularly nauseating people, using the image of stupidity to pump them for information, convince them to let their guard down, or even set them up to take a fall.  As the story goes, Bertram actually dressed up like a giant bat on his first trip to America to expose a phony detective on the take from the mob, so a little bit of superhero-style melodrama isn't always out of the question, either.  But, sometimes it means getting into bad markets to subtly inject subversive messages into the discourse or to fleece the so-called 'centrists' or 'moderates' who don't seem to mind seeing the people in their midst harmed, because they don't want to rock the boat too much or risk their portfolios."

"So, then what's all - "

"OK, OK.  Skipping ahead a few generations to get to you.  Last night, I broke up a sweatshop in Little Javasu."  Mom gestures around and, suddenly, I actually look at the office around me, not decorated like the rest of the building, still bright, but no direct natural light, except maybe through little pipes poking out of the ceiling that look a bit like giant fiber-optic cables.  The workers, all with that same jewelry, all have their intense conversations with anywhere from one to three people who look to be in pretty bad shape, with a couple of dozen of them almost certainly from Javasu.  "It didn't go entirely as planned, and I actually needed to patronize one of your medics."

Off in the background, we can all half-listen to the news over a radio, something about police arresting actor Xavier Exley for connection with something unfortunate.  Wealthy white men getting arrested, what has the world come to, right?

"This is our company's *other* Human Resources department, the one that official applicants never see.  The folks from the sweatshop have spent the day coming through here, as well as the usual daily handful of people who our associates have found trafficked, abused, or otherwise harmed without much other recourse.  Down here, we make sure to place them in decent jobs with Wooster Amalgamated and other companies where we have an 'in.'  For the handful who face some immediate danger, we'll quietly move them somewhere else in the country with a cover identity, though we don't have the resources of an actual Witness Protection Program, and so usually borrow the identities of our own people who can afford to lie low for a while.  And those who are in a position to provide evidence against their abusers, we schedule court appointments and provide security for them.  Some also need housing, and we just so happen to have plenty of open rooms in the Arcology at any given time."

"Well, congratulations, it sounds like you have it all figured out."

She smirks at me.  "Our family and friends *have* worked on this for more than a century, after all.  Therefore, it has grown into a well-oiled machine, pardon the mixed metaphor, but any machine can stand improvement provided by the next generation, Bertie, children correlating to the future and all that."  Again, I feel the urge to point out, that seems like a pretty dramatic assertion for someone who *just* told me not to act dramatic.  "The time has come for the League to take on new leadership."

"Georgiana, don't act so dramatic.  You've scared the child."  Heh.  I could do without the "child" comment, but damn, Grandpa Robert knows where to hit hard.  As long as I've known him, despite the obsession with looking his best, he has acted like the social version of a jeweler, carefully inspecting the facets he needs to work with and, after careful consideration, taking one decisive *crack* that causes everything to break his way.

"Your mother meant new leadership, at least to the extent that anybody involved in this takes orders.  You can't really enforce any authority over a group of go-getters like this, so much as point in a direction and have a good enough reason for them to want to follow, or identify the best ideas and back them," Grandma Serafina corrects.  "You probably also wouldn't want to exert any control over them, since they do a fine job on their own.  You know the story about the marksman calling his target after taking the shot.  We never would have prevented the Kravonian genocide if one of our associates hadn't pulled us in."

Dad takes something shiny out of his pocket.  "Wait, what are you - ?"  He has something with that upside-down A, which I now see clearly, obviously takes its design from a stylized bat, the literal **Silver Bat**, maybe platinum, knowing my family, and my father pins it on my lapel.

"The insignia identifies you as part of the network, with code phrases to allow you to ask for and offer assistance when needed.  Many of the people we help want to pay it forward, and so we induct them into the League.  You've probably seen the rings on people in the city throughout your life and never noticed until you walked into the building, this morning to see so many at the same time."  Now that he mentions it, actually, it does look familiar.

He continues.  "We have watched your little network of care for a few years from afar, now, and the way it has grown and the way you haven't compromised on your mission shows that you have what it takes to take Wooster Amalgamated and the League into the future, while your mother and I go into semi-retirement to provide support when you need it.  If you'll come back up to the office, we'll handle the paperwork to give you control of the company and start training you."

"I'll also introduce you to some of the accountants, Bertie, because you need to put in some better work to better cover the tracks of those clinics.  Shell companies and big purchases don't do nearly enough as a shield.  A child could see through that.  Hell, a corporate journalist, even."

I almost jump out of my skin as Nana Isla speaks.  I completely forgot that she even came down here with us, her smirk hinting that wasn't accidental.  "But also, no pressure, Bertie.  If you want to keep feeding and healing people from the shadows and nothing else, it's not like we could be any prouder of you."

Well, I have to admit it, my family did it.  They managed surprised me.  This is not at *all* how I thought my birthday was going to go and for the first time in twenty years, I kind of want a nap.  Oh, well.  I guess that the time has finally come to get to work.
